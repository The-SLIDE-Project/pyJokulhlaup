\begin{abstract}

Ice-marginal lakes are an increasingly common feature of glacierised landscapes, and their drainage via sudden jökulhlaup can threaten downstream communities and infrastructure. Numerous efforts to model jökulhlaups have been made, though typically these models are one-dimensional; representing a single channel connected to a lake and unable to represent lateral jökulhlaup propogation through the subglacial system as a result. Here, we present an ice-marginal lake model coupled to a fully coupled model of subglacial hydrology and basal sliding, enabling simulation of time-evolving jökulhlaups within a 2D subglacial drainage system. In experiments on a synthetic domain, the model produces stable, recurrent jökulhlaup cycles, and glacier acceleration during flood onset followed by abrupt slowdown at peak flood discharge. Sensitivity testing highlights the efficiency of the distributed subglacial hydrology system as a key control on flood timing, peak discharge, and the basal sliding response. We also explore our model's ability to represent an observed record of jökulhlaup by applying the model to Isunnguata Sermia, west Greenland. With a parameter set describing a restricted subglacial hydrology system, our model  successfully reproduces observed flood recurrence intervals and lake-level variability over the 17-year modelling period. However, we underpredict peak discharge---likely as a result of unrepresented mechanisms such as ice uplift and lake water temperature variability. These results establish the coupling of a lake model to a 2D subglacial hydrology and ice dynamics model as a viable approach for multi-decadal jökulhlaup simulation, and present an extensible framework which can be further improved upon in the future. 

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ice-marginal lakes, glacier abutting water bodies dammed by ice, are increasing in size and frequency as a result of lake basin geometry changes driven by glacier thinning and retreat \citep{clague2012climate,shugar2020rapid,molg2021inventory}. Around Greenland alone, $>$3,300 ice-marginal lakes have been recorded \citep{carrivick2022ice}, many of which hold potential to repeatedly and catastrophically drain in flood events called jökulhlaup \citep{ng2003clague}. These events are pose significant risk for downstream communities \citep{mernild2008jokulhlaup}, and are increasingly important considerations for hydropower infrastructure \citep{ahlstrom2008evaluation,priebe2024glacial}. Jökulhlaups can impart significant influence on ice-dynamics \citep{carrivick2022ice,domgaard2024altimetry,harpur2025emerging,livingstone2025ice}, and Pleistocene jökulhlaups are considered large enough to have precipitated abrupt changes in global ocean circulation and climate \citep{teller1995history,clark2001freshwater,mangerud2004ice,sufke2022arctic}.

Numerous attempts to realise a numerical jökulhlaup theory have been made \citep[e.g.,][]{nye1976water,spring1981numerical,clarke1982glacier,ng1998mathematical,fowler1999breaking,ng2003clague,kessler2004testing,ng2007climatic,kingslake2013modelling}. \citet{nye1976water} presented the first thermomechanical description, in which an ice-dammed lake drains via a single semi-circular subglacial channel melted upwards into the ice base which is connected to the glacier terminus. During the first stage of a flood (the `rising limb' of a flood hydrograph) the cross-sectional area of the channel increases as heat dissipated by flowing water drives wall melting. A positive feedback between channel enlargement and the rate of melting causes escalating discharge and rapid lake drainage. As the lake drains, water pressure drops and the rate of channel enlargement falls and ice deformation leads to channel closure and flood termination (the `falling limb' of a flood hydrograph).

Numerical solution of the Nye model \citep[e.g.,][]{spring1981numerical,clarke1982glacier,ng1998mathematical,fowler1999breaking} highlighted the need for full coupling between the lake and the subglacial drainage system when recreating jökulhlaup hydrographs; lake depth must set pressure at the channel inlet, and water flux at the channel inlet must equal the lake outflow. However, these studies demonstrated that such coupling produces flood cycles which grow unstably. \citet{fowler1999breaking} established that stable and recurrent flood cycles can occur if the channel receives a uniform meltwater supply, the model includes spatial dependence of the channel variables, and there is a retrograde slope towards the lake (negative basic hydraulic gradient) which allows a seal to develop between floods. \citet{kingslake2013modelling} extended the \citet{fowler1999breaking} model by allowing the flood channel to exchange water along effective pressure gradients with an adjacent cavity drainage system to account for basal sliding. The \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model showed that bounded flood cycles can occur even in the presence of a basic positive hydraulic gradient, and further characterised the response of ice velocity to changes in effective pressure at different stages of the jökulhlaup cycle. This work has been subsequently extended to include variable extraglacial lake input \citep[e.g., precipitation and snowmelt][]{kingslake2015chaotic}, systems comprising connected chains of lakes \citep{stubblefield2019modeling}, transitions between distributed and channelised systems at the ice--lake contact \citep{schoof2020analysis} and variable lake basin geometry \citep{jenson2022long}. 

These coupled solutions of jökulhlaup have remained one-dimensional (1D), in the sense that they capture variations along a flood conduit or pathway defined as a line or curve at the ice-bed interface \citep[e.g.,][]{fowler1999breaking,kingslake2013modelling}. Though this level of description permits analysis of some complex dynamics \citep{kingslake2015chaotic,stubblefield2019modeling,schoof2020analysis}, it precludes a representation of lateral jökulhlaup propagation along the subglacial interface \citep{kingslake2013modelling}, and cannot describe the interaction of floodwater with the broader subglacial drainage network, ice motion, or basal topography (including where the lake margin meets the subglacial interface) in a spatially-extended manner. Significant advancements in the 2D representation of subglacial hydrology \citep[e.g.,][]{werder2013modeling,sommers2018shakti} and its coupling to ice dynamics \citep{gilbert2022consistent,cook2022fully}, means that 2D hydrology models are commonly successfully used to investigate observations in real-world domains \citep[e.g.,][]{dow2019role,ehrenfeucht2025Antarctic,narayanan2025simulating}. Enabled by these advancements, here we extend the work of \citet{kingslake2013modelling} to couple jökulhlaup with the subglacial drainage system and glacier motion, by using a sophisticated 2D subglacial hydrological model that accounts for basal sliding, and posing an ice-marginal lake (with evolving water depth) as a boundary condition. We experiment with this model first on a synthetic domain under controlled conditions, and then apply it to Isunnguata Sermia, an outlet glacier in West Greenland. The synthetic tests serve to characterise model sensitivity and establish parameter bounds, while the Greenland application assesses the model's ability to reproduce an observed record of ice-marginal lake fill--drain cycles.

\section{Model formulation}
We simulate subglacial hydrology through multiple jökulhlaup cycles by using the 2D \sloppy{Glacier Drainage System} (GlaDS) model \citep{werder2013modeling}, specifically the version of GlaDS as integrated into the Ice-sheet and Sea-level System Model \citep[ISSM; ][ \texttt{commit a9a3804}]{larour2012continental}. The ice-marginal lake, which includes no spatial variability, has a fixed area and lake depth through time which varies according to water flux at the lake/glacier boundary. To represent this, and the lake influence on ice-dynamics our model includes couplings between, i) the subglacial hydrological system and the ice-marginal lake, ii) the subglacial hydrological system and ice dynamics, and iii) the ice-marginal lake and ice dynamics (Fig.~\ref{FIG:1}). In Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS} we describe the ISSM-GlaDS model and relevant additional modifications to the version originally presented by \citet{werder2013modeling}. We describe the equations governing ice-marginal lake evolution in Section~\ref{methods:Lake}, and our method of numerical solution is given in Section~\ref{methods:Numeric}.

\subsection{Ice Flow and subglacial drainage models}\label{methods:GlaDS}

GlaDS is a widely used  2D continuum model of subglacial hydrology \citep{dow2019role,  mcarthur2023basal,hayden2023examining,hepburn2024organization,ehrenfeucht2025Antarctic}. In summary, GlaDS operates on an anisotropic mesh and includes a description of i) distributed flow through a linked-cavity network system represented as a continuous sheet with variable thickness, and ii) channelised flow through Röthlisberger channels that are located along element edges. Sheet elements exchange water with channels, and the cross-sectional area of these channels evolves through time due to the dissipation of potential energy, sensible heat exchange, and cavity closure rates due to viscous ice creep. Discharge in both the distributed ($\bm{q}_{\mathrm{s}}$, $\mathrm{m}^2 \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) and channelised system ($Q_{\mathrm{c}}$, $\mathrm{m}^{3} \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) is driven by gradients in the hydraulic potential, $\nabla \phi$ ($\mathrm{Pa}\,\mathrm{m}^{-1}$), with steeper hydraulic gradients driving enhanced water discharge. In GlaDS, $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ is always non-zero along every internal edge, though only a few reach a discernible discharge. For ease of visualisation we follow \citet{werder2013modeling} in declaring edges where $Q_{\mathrm{c}} \geq$1 m$^3$s$^{-1}$ to be `meaningful channels' (e.g., Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e, Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c, Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}--e). Water in both distributed and channelised drainage components is assumed to exist at the pressure melt point. An advantage of GlaDS for simulating jökulhalup pathways is that the location of these meaningful channels is automatically determined and may evolve throughout a simulation without any prior prescription, as done in previous 1D jökulhalup models. Boundary conditions are applied in GlaDS either as a prescribed flux or pressure, both of which are expressed in terms $\phi$. We refer interested readers to Appendix~\ref{Appendix:GlaDS} for an overview of key GlaDS equations, and to \citet{werder2013modeling} for a full description.

ISSM is a finite element higher-order ice-flow model which operates on anisotropic meshes and can be coupled to additional modelling components such as GlaDS \citep{larour2012continental}. In addition to the ice-marginal lake modifications, which we describe below (Section~\ref{methods:Lake}), the primary difference between the original GlaDS \citep{werder2013modeling} and the ISSM version used here is that rather than assuming water flow within the continuous sheet to be turbulent \citep[cf:][]{werder2013modeling}, we allow it to switch freely between laminar and turbulent flow according to the local Reynolds number, by implementing the \citet{hill2024improved} transition parametrisation. We also prevent cavity expansion by ice creep when effective pressure, $N < 0$ Pa ($N=p_{\mathrm{i}}-p_{\mathrm{w}}$, where $p_{\mathrm{i}}$ is ice pressure and $p_{\mathrm{w}}$ is water pressure) because GlaDS does not include mechanisms to represent uplift of basal ice (separation at the ice-bed interface) once water pressure exceeds ice overburden \citep[e.g.,][]{tsai2010model,schoof2012flotation,dow2015modeling}. Without a physical representation of uplift, allowing cavity expansion by ice creep when $N<0$ Pa is undesirable and has been shown to inhibit channel formation in overpressurised regions of the bed \citep{hill2024improved}.

%In addition to the ice-marginal lake modifications which we describe below (Section~\ref{methods:Lake}), we note two key differences between the original GlaDS \citep{werder2013modeling} and the ISSM version used here: i) rather than assuming water flow within the sheet to always be turbulent \citep[cf:][]{werder2013modeling}, we make use of the \cite{hill2024improved} transition parametrisation, whereby sheet flow switches between laminar and turbulent flow according to the local Reynolds number, and ii) following \cite{stevens2022tidewater}, we implemented a viscously deforming `elastic sheet layer' which crudely represents hydraulic jacking where water pressure exceeds ice overburden pressure (see Section~\ref{Appendix:GlaDS}). 

To capture the influence of ice-marginal lake drainage on basal sliding, we include a two-way coupling between ice flow and subglacial hydrology. The GlaDS derived effective pressure $N$ modifies basal velocity $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ ($\mathrm{m}\, \mathrm{a}^{-1}$) by governing frictional resistance at the ice-bed interface. In turn, the magnitude of $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ modifies the rate at which cavities open and thereby controls the thickness of the sheet (Eq.~\ref{appeqn: hs_cav}--\ref{appeqn: basal sliding}). These interactions featured in the \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model, but the use of ISSM--GlaDS here enables the resulting ice-dynamical variations to be fully captured in 2D. 

Coupling between ISSM and GlaDS is a relatively new development, and the influence of friction parameters remains the subject of ongoing research \citep[e.g.,][]{khan2024inland}, though model response is known to be strongly influenced by the choice of friction law \citep{choi2023impact}. We view this work as necessarily explorative, and we do not undertake a full analysis of the modelling interactions between subglacial hydrology and ice dynamics. Rather, we seek to capture the broad influence that ice-marginal lake drainage cycles have upon ice dynamics. Accordingly, we present results using two widely applied friction laws: i) the `Budd-style' friction law \citep{budd1984three} implemented in ISSM in terms of basal stress with the form:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:frictionBudd}
\begin{aligned}
\tau_{\mathrm{b}} = C^{2}_{\mathrm{b}} N^{\mathrm{r}} U_{\mathrm{b}}^{s} ,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $\tau_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the basal stress magnitude (Pa), $C_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the friction coefficient, $N$ is effective pressure, $U_{\mathrm{b}}$ is basal velocity magnitude, and both $r$ and $s$ are friction coefficients, and ii) a Schoof-style friction law \citep{schoof2005effect,gagliardini2007finite} with the form: 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:frictionSchoof}
\begin{aligned}
\tau_{\mathrm{b}}=\frac{C_{\mathrm{s}}^2 v_{\mathrm{b}}^m}{\left(1+\left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{s}}^2}{C_{\max } N}\right)^{1 / m} U_{\mathrm{b}}\right)^m},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $C_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the \textit{Schoof} friction coefficient, $m$ is a friction law exponent, and $C_{\mathrm{max}}$ is Iken's bound. We used the Budd-style friction law (Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionBudd}) in our synthetic experiments, and the Schoof-style friction law (Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionSchoof}) in the Greenland run. Following \citet{mcarthur2023basal} we limit $N$ to $\geq$3\% of ice overburden pressure with both friction laws to prevent instabilities as $N \to 0$ Pa. 





\subsection{Lake evolution}\label{methods:Lake}

In our model, a lake is strongly defined as a specific Dirichlet boundary condition (see below)  at $n$ vertices along the boundary of the ice-lake contact. Between neighbouring vertices which do and do not describe an ice-lake-contact boundary, the value of the Dirichlet boundary condition is interpolated.

As in previous studies, the lake depth, $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ ($\mathrm{m}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:1}), evolves according to the continuity equation:


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lakecontinuity}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\mathrm{d} l_{\mathrm{h}}}{\mathrm{d} t} = \frac{1}{A_{\mathrm{L}}}[Q_{\mathrm{in}}-Q_{\mathrm{r}}],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\noindent
where $A_\mathrm{L}$ is the lake area ($\mathrm{m}^2$), $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ ($\mathrm{m}^3\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) represents extraglacial lake input (e.g., rainfall, surface runoff) and $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ ($\mathrm{m}^3 \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) represents outflow from the lake (when $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is negative there is inflow into the lake). Here, we also include the water exchange between the lake and the sheet \citep[unlike][who isolated cavities from the lake]{kingslake2013modelling}, so that $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sumQr}
\begin{aligned}
Q_{\mathrm{r}}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( q_{\mathrm{s},i}(t) + Q_{\mathrm{c},i}(t) \right),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\noindent
so that $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is the sum of the normal component of the continuous sheet discharge $\mathbf{q}_{\mathrm{s}}$, integrated along the $i$th boundary edge, $q_{\mathrm{s},i}$  ($\mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$), and channel discharge, $Q_{\mathrm{c},i}$ at the ice-lake contact vertices. As a boundary condition at and between vertices along the ice-lake interface, hydrostatic lake pressure fixes the hydraulic potential, $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ to be: 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lakephi}
\begin{aligned}
\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}(t) = \rho_{\mathrm{w}}gz_{\mathrm{b}} + \rho_{\mathrm{w}}gl_{\mathrm{h}}(t) ,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

at all times, where $g$ is gravitational acceleration ($\mathrm{m}\,\mathrm{s}^{-2}$) and $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ is bed elevation ($\mathrm{m \ a.s.l}$). Therefore, lake filling/draining alters $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ and thus $\nabla \phi$ and sheet/channel discharge along the ice-lake contact boundary (see Eq.~\ref{appeqn:gladsqs} \&~\ref{appeqn: Qc}). In the case of an ice--lake contact defined on a single vertex (as in the synthetic experiments described below), the sheet discharge in Eq.~\ref{eq:sumQr} is integrated over the combined half-edge length either side of the ice--lake contact. This maintains consistent dimensions (flux per unit length) and avoids double-counting in assembly. Finally, because GlaDS assumes that subglacial water temperatures are equal to the pressure melting point, we assume that the lake water also exists at the pressure melting point. The consequences of this decision are explored in Section~\ref{discussion:limitations}.

\subsection{Method of numerical solution}\label{methods:Numeric}

At each new timestep, we solve for $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ using a Picard fixed-point iteration scheme comprising an outer and inner loop. In the outer loop, we integrate Eq~\ref{eq:lakecontinuity} forward in time to estimate $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ at the current (i.e., new) timestep given the current estimate of $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (initialised from the previous timestep), using this $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ to set $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$. In the inner loop, GlaDS solves for the domain-wide $\phi$ until it satisfies a set convergence criteria, updating $N$ as it does so \citep[see:][]{werder2013modeling,hill2024improved}. Following the inner loop converging, and given the estimated field of $\phi$ ($X,Y$), the model updates $h_{\mathrm{s}}$, $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$, and $q_{\mathrm{s}}$, before summing $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ and $q_{\mathrm{s}}$ at lake boundary nodes to give a new estimate of $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Eqn.~\ref{eq:sumQr}). The outer loop iterates until the relative change in the two-norm of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ between successive iterations falls below a given tolerance.

\section{Model application to a synthetic domain}\label{application:synth}

First, we conduct synthetic simulations with the model to establish its consistency with \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, and furthering their results. We use a 10$\times$3 km rectangular domain (Figure~\ref{FIG:2}c--f), discretised on a 2D triangular mesh with a characteristic edge length of 100 m ($n_{\mathrm{vertices}}$=2486). The bed elevation, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ is characterised by a gentle slope ($\sin=0.0075$) dipping towards the terminus, and ice thickness follows a parabolic profile that increases from $H=50$ m at $X=0$ km to $H\approx155$ m at $X = 10$ km. An ice-marginal with $A_{\mathrm{L}}=5\ \mathrm{km}^{2}$lake was connected to a single vertex at the upglacier boundary where $X=10 \ \text{km}$. The simulations here are aimed at facilitating comparison with those of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, so we used a similar domain as theirs, which was chosen to be loosely analogous to the Merzbacher Lake and South Inylchek Glacier, Kyrgyztan \citep{ng2009temporal}. We connect the lake hydrologically to GlaDS at a single boundary vertex $(X, Y) = (10,\ 1.5)\ \text{km}$, isolating the rest of the lake margin from the subglacial drainage system. This was necessary because \citet{kingslake2013modelling} coupled the lake to a single flood channel only and did not couple the adjacent linked cavity system to the lake. 

Model variables are given in Table~\ref{tab:Var} and our synthetic model parameters are given in Table~\ref{tab:Synth}. The parameter dependency of GlaDS has been explored extensively \citep[e.g.,][]{dow2016modeling,dow2020totten,hill2024improved}. We limit our sensitivity experiments in Section~\ref{ss:sensitivity} below to parameters regarded as critical to subglacial drainage and known to be the largest sources of uncertainty in subglacial hydrology models \citep{werder2013modeling,hill2025computationally,hill2025emulator} which include: i) the channel and sheet conductivity terms $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ and $k_{\mathrm{s}}$, ii) the basal bump height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$; iii) the channel sheet width, $l_{\mathrm{c}}$; and iv) the cavity spacing, $l_{\mathrm{r}}$; as well as the terms which control the input of water into both the subglacial system: v) basal melt rate $M_{\mathrm{r}}$; and vi) the lake refill rate, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$. A reference simulation was conducted at the mid-range values of these parameters as listed in Table~\ref{tab:Synth}. Then, as is common practice \citep{dow2022role}, we varied one parameter at a time between a minimum and maximum bound (Table~\ref{tab:Synth}; informed both by previous work and the limits of numerical stability), in additional runs (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}). 

Each simulation was run from $t=0$ to 15 years at 30-minute timesteps. We used a Budd-style friction law (Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionBudd}) with $C_{\mathrm{b}} = 100$, $r = 1/4$, and $s = 1/4$ \citep[chosen to be consistent with][]{bindschadler1983importance,kingslake2013modelling}. The ice dynamic model was initialised with $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}} = 100,\mathrm{m,a}^{-1}$, with Dirichlet boundary conditions ($\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}} = 0,\mathrm{m\,a}^{-1}$) at the glacier terminus and stress-free Neumann boundary conditions elsewhere. The hydrology model was initialised with $\phi$ equivalent to 90\% of ice overburden pressure, and an initial sheet thickness, $h_{\mathrm{s}}$ equal to half the bump height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$. We prescribe $\phi$ at the terminus to be $\approx$1$\times$10$^{4}$ as our GlaDS Dirichlet boundary condition, though arbitrary, this does not qualitatively affect our results below. We impose zero water inflow as our Neumann boundary condition at the other three boundaries. The ice-marginal lake had an initial water height of 30 m ($l_{\mathrm{h}}$ at $t=0$). The evolution of the reference case through time is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}, and the sensitivity of lake height ($l_{\mathrm{h}}$), flood discharge ($Q_{\mathrm{r}}$), and basal velocity magnitude ($\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$) through time is shown in Figs.~\ref{FIG:3} \&~\ref{FIG:4}.

\subsection{Stable flood cycles}

In the reference simulation, the lake undergoes recurrent, stable, and asymmetric fill-drain cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}a). Drainage is associated with a recurring flood with stable maximum amplitude and duration (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}b). The lake only partially drains each flood cycle, and never reaches a height necessary to sustain flotation ($N=0$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}f). In the initial 5 years of transient evolution, when the model adjusts to initial conditions, peak flood discharge increases with each successive flood, and the lake reaches successively lower post-flood lowstands (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}a,b). Thereafter, the flood cycles reach a stable periodicity and clockwise hysteresis (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}f) with flood cycles occurring approximately every 1.5 years. As will be discussed in Section~\ref{discussion}, these simulated cycles are comparable to---yet with notable differences from---those of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}.

The evolution of the subglacial channel system through a single flood cycle is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e (an animated version is shown in SUPP MOVIE), and the width-averaged basal velocity response over the same flood cycle and into the following is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g. At the start of a flood cycle (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c), as the lake approaches its highstand, channelised drainage is limited and $\nabla\phi$ generally perpendicular to the ice surface slope. Close to the ice--lake contact ($X > \mathrm{9\ km}$), contours of hydraulic potential form ridge pointing downglacier from the ice--lake contact. In GlaDS, sheet discharge, $q_{\mathrm{s}}$, is directed perpendicular to these contours of $\nabla\phi$ and such ridges would induce sheet flow out of the lake. Four short ($<$100 m), low-discharge ($\geq$1 m$^{3}\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}$) channels remain open following formation during the previous flood cycle, three at the terminus and one at the ice--lake contact.

During the lake filling phase, increasing $l_\mathrm{{h}}$ drives $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ up (Eq.~\ref{eq:lakephi}), resulting in a steeper $\nabla \phi$ across the domain and promoting channelised drainage close to the ice--lake contact [SUPP MOVIE]. During the lake drainage phase, initiated once $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ begins to rapidly exceed the lake refill rate, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ (when $l_\mathrm{{h}} \approx \mathrm{75\ m}$ and $t \approx \mathrm{7.9 \, yrs}$) an arborescent channel structure originating from the lake extends across the full height of the domain, perpendicular to the contours of $\phi$, and deviating from the direction of ice-surface slope. Simultaneously, channels extend upglacier from the terminus, and contours of $\phi$ become increasingly convex downglacier. At the flood peak, channel tributaries coalesce into the three primary terminus channels which evacuate water from the system. As the lake level drops sharply during a flood, $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ drops, reducing $\nabla \phi$, and in turn reducing $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ below the level necessary to sustain channels across the domain, including at the ice--lake contact. Once $Q_{\mathrm{r}} < Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ the flood terminates and the cycle begins again. Each cycle takes $\approx$1.5 years with the rising and falling limb of the flood lasting $\approx$6 months. 

Basal velocity, $U_{\mathrm{b}}$ changes significantly during a flood cycle (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g) tracking the development of a significant channelised drainage system. The glacier accelerates during the flood initiation phase before channelised drainage is widely established, then decelerates abruptly as channels become well-established, $\approx$two months before the flood peak (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g). Ice flow acceleration propagates from the ice--lake contact towards the terminus, with maximum velocity sustained within 2 km of the ice--lake contact.

\subsection{Parameter sensitivity}\label{ss:sensitivity}


Fig.~\ref{FIG:3} plots the time series of lake height, $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ and lake discharge $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ from a total of 14 simulations across the maximum (red lines), and minimum (blue lines) parameter values, with the reference `\textit{mid-point}' simulation in grey. The majority of simulations yield stable and recurring asymmetric lake filling and drainage cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[i]--f[i]) with corresponding variations in channel discharge at the ice--lake contact, $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[ii)]--f[ii]). The parameter variations mostly strongly influence the amplitude of lake height and its rate of change, both of which influence the timing of flood initiation and peak flood discharge, with higher amplitude lake depth cycles leading to higher peak flood discharges \citep{ng2003clague}.

Increasing channel conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, in effect making channels more sensitive to $\nabla \phi$ (see Eq.~\ref{appeqn: Qc}), leads to lower lake highstands as the lake drains sooner. However, peak discharge is higher as the lake also drains more quickly and drains entirely. At maximum $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, there is a longer transient before the model reaches a fixed flood recurrence interval, draining at a higher frequency than the default value of $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ until $\sim 11 \ \mathrm{years}$ into the simulation at which point flood cycles occur at approximately the same interval as in the default case (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[ii]). Conversely, at minimum $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, the capacity of channels to drain the lake is inhibited and flood onset is delayed. Because channels grow slowly, peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is also dampened leading to relatively long flood recurrence intervals ($\sim 4\ \mathrm{years}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[ii]).

Increasing the sheet conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ increases the capacity of the sheet to accommodate water and delays transmission to channels, this results in reduced lake highstands and delayed flood initiation as the onset of classical channel growth is delayed. Reducing $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ increases the relative efficiency of channels and leads to more vigorous lake-fill drain cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}b[i]), with shorter and higher discharge floods as a result (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}b[ii]). Changing $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ has a similar effect to reducing $k_{\mathrm{s}}$, although at the maximum bump height, the sheet becomes so efficient that it can accommodate all lake discharge and within 1 year the lake reaches a stable equilibrium where $Q_{\mathrm{r}} = Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}b[i]). 

Changing the contributing width of the sheet to the channel ($l_{\mathrm{c}}$) or the representative horizontal spacing of cavities ($l_{\mathrm{r}}$) alters the relative efficiency of channelised and sheet drainage. Reducing $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ and increasing $l_{\mathrm{c}}$ reduces the lake highstand and lowers peak discharge, and vice versa when increasing $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ and reducing $l_{\mathrm{c}}$. Finally, increasing input of water into either the glacier via basal melt rate, $M_{\mathrm{r}}$ or lake via $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ enhances the amplitude of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ oscillations and in turn, peak flood discharge. 

In all simulations, the maximum lake height remains below the maximum ice thickness (150 m) and does not surpass the effective pressure necessary to initiate ice flotation ($N=0$). In most simulations the lake does not reach a lowstand below $\sim$10 m, only draining completely when $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ = 0.2 m$^{3/2}$kg$^{-1/2}$, and when $h_{\mathrm{b}}=0.05\ \mathrm{m}$.

\section{Model application to Isunnguata Sermia, Greenland}\label{application:greenland}

Isunnguata Sermia drains a catchment of $\approx\!16\,000\ \mathrm{km}^2$ in West Greenland (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}a--c). In its terminus region (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c), its flow axis is characterised by a deep, flow-parallel bedrock trough with an anabranching structure in its upper reaches. The glacier terminates into a sub-aerial proglacial stream system. A recently described  $\approx\!3\ \mathrm{km}^2$, $\approx\!100\ \mathrm{m}$ deep ice-dammed lake \citep{livingstone2025ice} abuts the northern margin of Isunnguata Sermia's tongue. The lake drains subglacially with a periodicity of 1--3 years, having undergone at least 12 fill-drain cycles between 1987--2024 \citep[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g]{livingstone2025ice}. There are two ice-contact boundaries between Isunnguata Sermia and the ice-marginal lake, a prominent ice cliff boundary along the western margin of the lake through which the lake appears to drain, and an intermittent boundary along the eastern margin that is established once lake level is sufficiently high \citep{livingstone2025ice}.

\subsection{Model setup} \label{application:setup}

We discretised our Isunnguata Sermia domain \citep[derived from][]{lindback2015subglacial} using an anistropic mesh with an edge length of 150 m close to terminus, increasing to 2 km close to the upper margin of our domain at the approximate ELA (at \( H \approx 1600\ \text{m} \)). The mesh was further refined to a typical edge length of 50 m close to the ice-marginal lake (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). Bedmachine v5 was used for $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ and $H$ \citep[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}b,c]{morlighem2017bedmachine,Morlighem_Et_Al_2022}. Our domain was clipped to exclude areas of the glacier where $H<100 \, \mathrm{m}$. Basal velocity, $U_{\mathrm{b}}$, was initialised from the 2021 MEaSUREs annual surface velocity mosaic \citep{MEASURES}. Geothermal heat flux was taken from \citet{shapiro2004inferring}, and basal melt rates were taken from \citet{karlsson2021first}. The flow-law rate factor $A$ within the ice-column was derived using surface ice temperatures from \citet{ettema2009higher} and assuming a temperature-dependent Arrhenius relation following \citet{cuffey2010physics}. 

Our model includes multiple boundary conditions in order to represent Isunnguata Sermia. For the hydrological solution, we imposed two distinct Dirichlet boundary conditions at three locations: i) at the two ice--lake contact boundaries $phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ was allowed to evolve with $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ (see Eq.~\ref{eq:lakephi}) and ii) a fixed atmospheric boundary was imposed at the glacier terminus. At all other vertices, we imposed Neumann (zero-inflow) boundary conditions. The ice--lake contacts were manually delineated: seven vertices were identified at the western lake boundary and at the eastern margin we identified the lowest elevation vertex within a group of vertices close to the observed stream. At all ice-lake contact vertices we fixed bed elevation equal to the minimum lake elevation (257.9 m a.s.l). Because our model assumes a fixed lake area ($A_{\mathrm{L}}=3\ \mathrm{km}^2$) and cannot dynamically represent intermittent lake-glacier contact along the eastern margin, prescribing the same boundary conditions at the eastern ice--lake contact as along the western ice--lake contact provides a simple and consistent boundary treatment and ensures water can exchange between the lake and subglacial system at both places. At the glacier terminus we set $\phi$ equal to atmospheric pressure at the lowest elevation boundary vertex (cyan triangle in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). Within the ice-dynamic solution, the boundary condition velocity is given by the initialisation $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ (Dirichlet), except at the western ice--lake contact where velocity is allowed to evolve freely (Neumann). 

Model simulation necessitates knowledge of the spatial field of basal friction coefficient, $C$, which we assumed to be time-invariant. Because we have an observational constraint on ice-surface velocity, we used a Schoof-style friction law (see Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionSchoof}, Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}), inverting for the basal friction coefficient using an L-curve scheme \citep[following; ][]{wolovick2023regularization}. A Schoof-style friction law has been shown to more faithfully represent ice-dynamics with a GlaDS derived $N$ than the Budd-style friction law \citep{mcarthur2023basal}. We ran an initial inversion for $C$ using an empirically-derived $N$ (assuming $N=p_{i}$), before running a simple GlaDS-only model run over 10 years, which did not include an ice-marginal lake, surface melt or two-way coupling to ice dynamics. This allowed us to derive a more realistic and spatially variable estimate field of $N$ from which a second inversion for basal friction could be obtained. Such an approach has previously been demonstrated to yield more faithful basal inversions in a coupled hydrology model \citep[see;][]{ehrenfeucht2023seasonal,mcarthur2023basal}. 

As climate forcing, we used the 10 km resolution daily Modèle Atmosphérique Régional (MAR, v3.14) runoff and accumulation data \citep{fettweis2017reconstructions} between 2007--2025 inclusive. Whereas we kept $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ fixed in the synthetic experiments (Section~\ref{application:synth}), here the extraglacial water input to the lake is likely to vary with time as a function of air temperature \citep{ng2007climatic,ng2009temporal}. To account for this, we use a simple temperature index \citep[as in][]{kingslake2015chaotic}, where $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ is given by: 


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Tempindex}
\begin{aligned}
Q_{\mathrm{in}} =
\begin{cases}
\mathrm{max}(kT,1), & T > 0 \\
1, & T \le 0
\end{cases}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where the temperature-index coefficient $ k $ is equal to $2\ \mathrm{m}^{3}\mathrm{s}^{-1}\mathrm{K}^{-1}$. We set $Q_{\mathrm{in}} \geq 1 \ \mathrm{m}^{3}\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ to capture positive changes in lake elevation observed between melt seasons in Fig~\ref{FIG:5}g. 

Since our objective was to assess the model's ability to reproduce the observed record of fill-drain cycles of an ice-marginal lake in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g, we focus on achieving a simulation that yields the closest quantitative match. Guided by insights from the synthetic experiments (Section~\ref{application:synth}), we chose the final parameters (Table~\ref{tab:Greenland}) as follows: a decrease in channel conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, to 0.02 $\mathrm{m}^{\frac{3}{2}}\mathrm{kg}^{-\frac{1}{2}}$, an increase in sheet conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ to 0.05 $\mathrm{Pa\ s}^{-1}$, a decrease in the basal bump height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ to 0.05 m, $e_{\mathrm{v}}$ to 0.01, and changes to both the channel sheet width, $l_{\mathrm{c}}$, and cavity spacing, $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ to 10 m. Compared to the synthetic case, we also increased the englacial void ratio, $e_{\mathrm{v}}$, to 0.01. This had little influence on our synthetic case when tested to the same limits. However, in our Greenland experiments, increasing $e_{\mathrm{v}}$ dampened the model sensitivity to sudden changes in meltwater input to the system and prevented unreasonably high water pressures ($>300\%$ of overburden pressure) in isolated pockets close to the domain boundary. Across sensitivity tests, parameter variations produced changes in the timing and amplitude of modelled outputs without altering the qualitative drainage system behaviour.

We simulated the Greenland model forward in time from 1987 to 2025 (for 38 years at a 5 minute timestep). As the main goal of our modelling, this simulation consists of three phases, the first of which involved running the model (for twenty years of model time) to reach a winter steady state where the 95\textsuperscript{th} percentile difference in sheet thickness between successive timesteps was below $1\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m}$. During the steady state phase, we allowed the ice-marginal lake to freely evolve from an initial height of $l_{\mathrm{h}}=70\ \mathrm{m}$, the mean lake height between 1987--2024 \citep{livingstone2025ice}. The steady state phase allowed the model to reach a condition approximating the anticipated wintertime hydrological conditions in which there is limited channel activity and water pressures across the domain are close to the flotation fraction ($p_{\mathrm{w}}\approx 0.7--0.9 \, p_{\mathrm{i}}$). We then ran a `spin-up' phase from 1997--2007 where we introduced a climate forcing that by gradually increasing by increasing the 2007 runoff by 10\% each year. Following this spin-up, we ran an unconstrained phase from 2007--2025 where we apply the full climate forcing and whose results are compared with the 2007--2025 lake level record. In the spin-up and unconstrained phase, results are stored every 72 hours.

\subsection{Flood cycles in Greenland} \label{application:results}

The combined spin-up and unconstrained modelling phase is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}d--g. At the peak of a flood, using model year 2022 as an example, the simulated drainage system of Isunnguata Sermia is characterised by a channel that occupies the central trough and extends $>$45 km upglacier (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). The majority of surface melt is routed through this `primary drainage axis' towards the glacier terminus, and an anabranching tributary system is visible $\sim$20 km upstream. Near the terminus, a second drainage axis, comprising a high discharge central channel and two lower discharge subsidiary channels, connects the western ice--lake contact to the primary drainage axis, converging $\sim$ 2 km from the terminus.

The spin-up phase (dashed line in Fig~\ref{FIG:5}d--f) is characterised by three cycles of lake filling and partial drainage, each reaching successively higher lake highstands and having a shorter flood recurrence period than the previous one; these cycles track the development of a catchment wide subglacial drainage system induced by successively more intense melt summers (Fig~\ref{FIG:5}d). From 2007 onwards, once the model is subject to the full extent of annual melt-forcing, the lake repeatedly fills and drains with a recurrence interval between floods of $\sim$2--4 years. Lake fill-drain cycles are characterised by slow-rising limbs (between jökulhlaup) and abrupt drawdowns (jökulhlaup), though their shape are irregular. Lake drainages typically occur at the end of a melt season. Lake levels then remain low during the subsequent winter, before filling in an irregular sawtooth fashion \citep[as in][]{ng2009temporal}, with the lake filling more rapidly during summer melt seasons and remaining stable, or lowering slightly, during the intervening winter period. Lake drainage is triggered following the second or third melt season, and the cycle begins anew. 

Simulated jökulhlaup reach a peak discharge of $Q_{\mathrm{r}}\approx$100--200~m$^{3}$~s$^{-1}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}e). Each modelled jökulhlaup lasts 3--4 months, far exceeding the typical 5--8 day events observed at Isunnguata Sermia \citep{livingstone2025ice}. As a result of the long duration jökulhlaups, our typical modelled peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}\approx$100--200~m$^{3}$~s$^{-1}$ is also markedly lower than that of the 2022 jökulhlaup described by \citet{livingstone2025ice}, which had an estimated minimum mean discharge of 480~m$^{3}$~s$^{-1}$ and a likely considerably higher instantaneous peak.

The timing and magnitude of modelled lake highstands in our transient phase aligns well with that in the observed record, capturing variability in both flood timing and the proportion of lake drainage. Eight lake highstands are evident in the observed record (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g) between 2007--2024, seven of which corresponds with a simulated lake highstand to within $\sim$2 months and $\pm$ 20 m (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f). Further, there is also good overall agreement in the magnitude of lake lowstands, with the model matching observations of partial (e.g., 2010--2011) and complete lake drainage events (e.g., 2007--2008). This match is not perfect (e.g., 2013) and may be influenced by gaps in the observational record \citep{livingstone2025ice}. 

The evolution of channels during a typical jökulhlaup cycle (2020--2023) is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}. On reaching lake highstand in 2019, the development of a significant channel connection between the lake and the primary drainage axis enables jökulhlaup onset and the drop in lake level (Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}b). As the $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ and $\nabla\phi$ lowers at the western ice--lake contact, the lake draining channel is starved of supply and shuts down by creep closure. At the lake lowstand, once $phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ is close to atmospheric pressure, drainage into the lake from the eastern ice--lake contact begins in summer 2020 (Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}c) and the lake quickly fills to $\sim 50\ \mathrm{m}$. In the 2021 summer (Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}d) and 2022 summer, no significant channels develop at the eastern ice--lake contact, and $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ accounts for the rise in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$. By the end of the 2022 melt season (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}, \& Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}e), a relatively high discharge jökulhlaup drains the lake via a series of channels connected to the primary drainage axis. Though seven vertices along the western ice--lake contact are connected to the lake, discharge at the lake boundary is concentrated within a single high-discharge channel. Midway along the flood path, $\approx$2.5 km from the lake, this channel diverges and branches into multiple lower discharge channels running sub-parallel to the main lake-draining channel and perpendicular to $\nabla \phi$, a behaviour also evident in the synthetic runs (see Section~\ref{application:synth} \& Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e).

In panels c and g, red colours indicate ice acceleration towards the terminus during a jökulhlaup; in panels d and h, blue indicates acceleration towards the lake and red indicates acceleration away from the lake. Observations additionally identify a southward acceleration in $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ close to the glacier terminus that is not reproduced in our model.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

The model presented here, which couples a time-evolving ice-marginal lake to a 2D representation of subglacial hydrology and basal sliding for the first time, corroborates many of the findings of \citet{kingslake2013modelling} from their 1D model. The lake gradually fills at a rate governed by the magnitude of the extraglacial input rate, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ and jökulhlaup initiate once lake depth, $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ reaches between 50--80$\%$ of ice thickness, $H$, corresponding to between 55--90$\%$ of ice overburden pressure. The characteristic stable and recurrent jökulhlaup cycles are pervasive across both the reference case (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}) and the majority of sensitivity tests (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}). Similarly, our model produces an overall response in basal velocity, $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$, to flood propagation resembling that of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}. Ice near to the lake accelerates markedly during flood growth and abruptly slows down during flood recession after peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g \&~\ref{FIG:4}). Such a velocity pattern has been observed during jökulhlaup at Gorenergletscher, Switzerland \citep{sugiyama2007reversal} and Skaftá, Iceland \citep{magnusson2007impact,einarsson2016spectrum,einarsson2017subglacial}. 

The overall agreement between the synthetic results in this work and that of earlier work is unsurprising, the lakes are governed by closely related equations and the underlying hydrology models assume broadly similar physics. However, our inclusion of a spatially variable (2D) hydrology solution, and our decision to permit hydraulic connectivity between the lake and the adjacent linked cavities, do present behaviours not evident or possible to explore with the 1D \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model. In their model, a single channel of unit length is connected to an ice-marginal lake. The channel itself is connected to a series of far-field linked cavities (with no spatial variation), and the two drainage components maintain independent effective pressure. Water exchange between the drainage elements occurs at a rate proportional to the pressure differential \citep[as in][]{hewitt2008seasonal}. Crucially, in the \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model, the lake is hydraulically isolated from the linked cavity system. 

In the model here, with a spatially variable representation of subglacial hydrology fields, pressure continuity is maintained across channels and the immediately adjacent continuous sheet; water exchange occurs at the rate necessary to maintain this condition and it is $\nabla \phi$ across neighbouring drainage elements that drives overall water flow. At the ice--lake contact, where $\phi_{contact}$ evolves through time (Eq.~\ref{eq:lakephi}), we maintain pressure continuity by allowing the linked cavities to exchange water with the lake, with sheet discharge integrated over an edge length centred on the single ice--lake contact (see Section~\ref{methods:Lake}). We consider our assumption of water exchange between the continuous sheet and the lake to be more physically plausible than the isolated cavity assumption of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}. This assumption has been shown to match well to observations of jökulhlaup elsewhere, when flux is prescribed across the ice--lake contact \citep[e.g.,][]{flowers2004coupled}.

The clearest distinction between our model and that of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, is in the divergent response to changes in basal melt, $M_{\mathrm{r}}$ \citep[denoted by cavity water supply, $M_{\mathrm{c}}$, in ][]{kingslake2013modelling}. In their model, an increase in basal melt reduces the peak flood $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$, whereas here, increasing the basal melt rate (substantially, from a base of 1 m a$^{-1}$ to 5 m a$^{-1}$) doubles peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}f[ii]). In the \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model higher basal melt lowers the effective pressure in the linked cavities, accelerates cavity--channel exchanges, promotes earlier channel growth and triggers lake drainage at lower lake levels, with a lower peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$. In our model, with a lake connected to the continuous sheet, elevated basal melt lowers the system-wide effective pressure, reduces $\nabla \phi$ at the ice--lake contact, inhibits lake leakage into the glacier and allows the lake to both fill more quickly and reach a higher $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ before drainage. Once triggered, the greater water supply sustains enlarged channels with a higher peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ that drains the lake to a lower $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ compared to the reference basal melt rate case. This effect in our model is asymmetric; reducing $M_{\mathrm{r}}$ below the baseline has negligible effect on flood recurrence interval or peak discharge (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}f[ii]), suggesting that below a threshold basal melt rate the system effective pressure is governed primarily by the lake drainage cycle itself rather than by the ambient melt supply, and the effective pressure (and in turn basal sliding) becomes effectively decoupled from meltwater availability at the bed. Our results indicate that in glacier--lake systems characterised by low basal meltwater availability, lake dynamics may become the dominant control on glacier sliding velocity over time.

Our results highlight drainage efficiency as a key control on jökulhlaup characteristics and basal sliding response. In our synthetic experiments, the lake drains at a lower highstand to a higher lowstand, with a longer, less intense flood if the efficiency of the distributed sheet is raised relative to the channelised system; for example, by raising the sheet conductivity ($k_{\mathrm{s}}$ Fig.~\ref{3}b), raising the basal bump height ($h_{\mathrm{b}}$Fig.~\ref{3}c), or decreasing cavity spacing ($l_{\mathrm{r}}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}e). Longer duration, less intense jökulhlaups drive higher and more sustained increases in $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$. With a longer rising limb, greater subglacial discharge is accommodated within the sheet, sustaining the low effective pressure, $N$ necessary to enable fast ice flow. Conversely, when channelised drainage is relatively efficient (e.g., Fig.~\ref{FIG:4}a,d,f), jökulhlaups are shorter in duration and peak drainage is higher. In turn, the $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ response is shorter and lower in peak velocity because efficient drainage develops more easily, increasing effective pressure over wide-tracts of the the glacier bed and inhibiting basal sliding. The parameters which control drainage efficiency in GlaDS and other comparable hydrology models are known to be highly uncertain \citep{werder2013modeling}. Our results further highlight the drainage system efficiency as a key uncertainty requiring improved observational constraint.

Our study of the lake--glacier system by using a 2D hydrology model (coupled to ice dynamics) yields insights beyond the reach of 1D set-ups. First, subglacial flood channel locations are not predefined in our model; they emerge as part of the solution (Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}). In the synthetic case, the local control of the lake on $\nabla \phi$ generates channels offset by 20--40$^{\degree}$ relative to the central axis of the glacier (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e). This lateral expansion of the jökulhlaup through the subglacial system results in a distinctive pattern of $\nabla\phi$ and an arborescent channel propagation which expands outwards from the central ice--lake contact position during a flood before collapsing into a few flow-parallel channels close to the terminus. This behaviour contrasts sharply with the ice-flow parallel channels expected in the absence of significant lateral topographic variation whereby the basic hydraulic potential is governed by ice surface slope alone \citep[e.g., see Figure 2 in][]{werder2013modeling}. Lateral propagation of flood water within the subglacial system during a jökulhlaup has been invoked as the cause of short-term lateral deviations in GPS measurements towards the ice-margins during jökulhlaups at Gorenergletscher, Switzerland \citep{sugiyama2007reversal}.

Coupling ice-marginal lake evolution to a 2D description of subglacial hydrology is an essential advance over previous work, because the resulting model can be applied to specific glaciers to study jökulhlaup behaviour in a realistic manner. Model application to Isunnguata Sermia (Section~\ref{application:greenland}), shows that the evolution of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ through time in our model (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f) closely matches the observed record reported by \citet[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:5},g]{livingstone2025ice}. Between 2007 and mid 2024, eight significant floods from the ice-marginal lake are recorded from remote sensing records (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g). Of these floods, seven are hindcasted by our model (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f), closely agreeing with observations in terms of timing and the amplitude of change in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$. 

Variability in the timing and character of jökulhlaup in our Isunnguata Sermia experiment is a direct consequence of coupling a lake to a 2D subglacial drainage system and forcing the system with realistic, time-varying climate record (see Section~\ref{application:greenland}) . Climate variability through time modulates the system-wide $\nabla \phi$ (and also changes lake input, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$, see Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}d), thereby reshaping the effective pressure field and the local capacity of the drainage system to transmit lake water. In lower-order models, similar variability in flood discharge arises when a transient climate is imposed \citep{kingslake2015chaotic, ng2009temporal}. Climate input variability propagates through the subglacial drainage system to produce nonlinear variability in jökulhlaup timing and peak discharge (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}e--f). The lack of such variability in jökulhlaup with consistent forcing in our synthetic experiments, combined with the close correspondence between modelled and observed lake behaviour at Isunnguata Sermia, suggests that this variability is an emergent feature of our coupling of a lake to a 2D subglacial system, rather than a model artefact. 

As well as a close agreement in terms of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$, our results corroborate additional observations from Isunnguata Sermia. Recently, a series of elevation anomalies have been identified close to the glacier terminus that are interpreted to have been caused by the filling and draining of subglacial lakes \citep{livingstone2019brief,livingstone2025ice}. Three subglacial lakes lie due south of the primary drainage axis \citep[Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c, \&~\ref{FIG:6}b--e][]{livingstone2019brief} and a fourth, more recently identified \citep{livingstone2025ice}, lies to the southwest of the ice-marginal lake (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c, \&~\ref{FIG:6}b--e). Floodwater from the ice-marginal lake likely triggered the observed 2019 surface lowering associated with the fourth subglacial lake \citep{livingstone2025ice}. In our model, the predicted jökulhlaup flowpath passes directly through this subglacial lake (as shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c \&~\ref{FIG:6}b--e) supporting the hypothesis that ice-marginal lake drainage is a triggering mechanism for subglacial lake drainage. 

Finally, our model results reproduce some of the observations relating to ice-dynamics at Isunnguata Sermia. Both our model and observations show acceleration along the primary flow direction during a flood and a component of acceleration towards the lake concentrated along the western ice--lake contact (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}). \citet{livingstone2025ice} attributed the observed acceleration to i) ice inflow into the lake as a result of a sudden drop in water level and change in force balance at the ice front, and ii) a reduction in basal friction as the sudden influx of flood water overpressurises remaining channels and the nascent distributed system becoming re-established at the end of the melt season. The boundary conditions in our ice-dynamic solution remain fixed through time and we do not simulate the change in frontal forces as the lake drops during a flood; in effect, our Neumann boundary condition corresponds to a permanently empty lake (see Section~\ref{application:setup}). As a result we overestimate the background $\mathrm{v}_{\amthrm{b}}$ into the lake (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}b) and underestimate the anomaly associated with lake drainage (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}d). Nonetheless, our modelling does reproduce limited acceleration into the lake during a flood as a result of low effective pressures close to the ice--lake contact during a jökulhlaup. 


\subsection{Model shortcomings and future outlook}\label{discussion:limitations}

% In doing so, our results provide additional insights into the limitations of both our approach, and  into more general, structural limitations with a model such as GlaDS. 

In this work, we the assess ability of a continuum model of subglacial hydrology to represent observable jökulhlaups. The primary discrepancy between our model and observations is that, as in the earlier 1D work \citep[e.g.,][]{kingslake2013modelling}, our experiments produce unrealistically long jökulhlaup durations extending multiple months. This stands in clear contrast to the days--weeks more typically associated with jökulhlaups \citep[e.g.,][]{bjornsson1992jokulhlaups,bjornsson2010understanding,einarsson2016spectrum,magnusson2026subglacial} and is much longer than the 5--10 day jökulhlaup duration observed at Isunnguata Sermia itself \citep{livingstone2025ice}. Results in Section~\ref{application:results} are presented from one model run at Isunnguata Sermia, chosen as the best match to lake level through time (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}); parameter testing was not exhaustive, and untested combinations may improve agreement with jökulhlaup duration. However, we expect the discrepancy to be attributable, at least in part, to shortcomings in our modelling setup (Sections~\ref{methods:GlaDS}--\ref{methods:Lake}) and in GlaDS more generally \citep{werder2013modeling}. 

As a continuum model, representing smoothly distributed drainage properties, GlaDS is not ideally suited to represent rapid, localised changes in subglacial water pressure or hydraulic connectivity \citep{rada2018channelized,hill2025emulator}. In ISSM-GlaDS, there is no physical representation of elastic uplift of ice once water pressure exceeds ice overburden pressure. This, together with our decision to prevent cavity expansion when effective pressure is negative (effectively fixing maximum sheet thickness to the basal bump height), means that our model is unable to replicate the instantaneous changes in sheet transmissivity expected once water pressure exceed ice overburden pressure \citep[e.g.,][]{tsai2010model,schoof2012flotation,dow2015modeling}. Work by \citet{flowers2004coupled}, albeit with a prescribed rate of flux from a subglacial lake, found that an elastic model able to represent sheet exceeding tens of metres in thickness can explain rapid escalations in jökulhlaup discharge over hours--days. Recent repeat satellite observations identified metres of uplift along a jökulhlaup pathway, suggesting that instantaneous ice uplift in a high water-pressure regime is crucial to enabling high jökulhlaup discharge \citep{magnusson2026subglacial}. A simple model of elastic deformation has been included in similar hydrology models elsewhere \citep[e.g.,][]{stevens2022tidewater}, and is worth exploring in future work. Here, with no physically-informed representation of rapid cavity expansion, our model is unable to reproduce this specific mode of fast flood onset and instead floods primarily evolve as a result of channel expansion. 

An additional limitation of our work is the assumption that water in the subglacial system, and crossing the ice--lake contact, is always at pressure melting point (Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}). Previous work \citep[e.g.,][]{clarke2003hydraulics,ng2007climatic,carrivick2017ice} has established high water temperature increases the energy available for thermal channel expansion, resulting in higher peak discharge and shorter duration floods \citep[see Fig.6A in][]{carrivick2017ice}. Without a detailed measurement of lake temperatures in our Greenland experiment, we effectively assume that the temperature of water leaving the lake across the ice--lake contact is also at the pressure melting point. In an analogous supraglacial lake setting, and using the \citet{spring1981numerical} equations for channel enlargement, \citet{werder2010hazard} found that increasing the lake temperature from 1$^{\degree}$C to 4$^{\degree}$C increases peak discharge from 380 $\mathrm{m}^3 \, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ to 960 $\mathrm{m}^3 \, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$. 

Finally, we made additional assumptions which influence our Greenland model outcomes and likely dampen the peak flood discharge. Here, lake area, $A_{\mathrm{l}}$ remains fixed in time, reducing the lake to a vertically-walled reservoir and ensuring that the scaling relationship between lake flux and lake depth remains constant. This simplification likely biases our lake drawdown rates towards being too slow; in reality as a lake drains its areal footprint decreases which would amplify the rate of lake level decrease during a jökulhlaup. In our ice dynamics model, we fix ice thickness through time. This was a deliberate modelling choice made in the abscense of a detailed understanding of lake calving dynamics to avoid the unphysical build up of ice at the lake boundary where surface mass balance alone is unable to account for observed mass balance changes. As a result, we do not capture the negative trend in peak $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ which is attributed to glacier thinning close to the terminus of Isunnguata Sermia \citep{livingstone2025ice}. Glacier thinning, and a reduction in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ necessary to initiate flooding, may contribute to the short return interval of the 2023 flood \citep{livingstone2025ice}, which is notable as the only flood in the observed record without a corresponding match in the modelled record. Future work will seek to implement a calving law, which will then allow ice thickness evolution necessary to account for changes in $H$ through time. Additionally, as described in Section~\ref{discussion}, our Neumann boundary condition at the western lake margin remains constant in time, effectively corresponding to a permanently empty lake. Recent work with a prescribed lake depth record has shown changing lake level can promote complex feedbacks in lake-terminating glacial systems as a result of the loading force a water body imparts on a vertical ice front \citep{pramanik2026modelling}. 

Our Greenland experiment parameters (Section~\ref{application:greenland}, Table~\ref{tab:Greenland}) provide additional insight into the limitations of our approach. Reproducing the amplitude and periodicity of lake fill--drain cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f,g) requires relatively low sheet and channel conductivities; a low basal bump height; and a relatively high englacial void ratio. Each parameter falls within previously established bounds \citep[e.g.,][]{werder2013modeling,downs2018dynamic,hill2025emulator} but are collectively indicative of a hydraulically inefficient subglacial system with high englacial water storage. Physically, an inefficient system restricts channel development and ensures that jökulhlaup only trigger once the hydrostatic pressure at the ice--lake contact approaches $N=0$. The high englacial void ratio then buffers water pressure during flood propagation, producing weeks--months long flood durations (e.g., Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}e,f). We suggest that water storage as a result of the high englacial void ratio is partially compensating for the absent physics described above; elastic uplift and thermally enhanced channel melt would both act to accelerate jökulhlaup propagation. This reflects a broader challenge in applying continuum subglacial hydrology models to investigate glaciological observations. Parameter choices can compensate for missing physical processes and allow models to reproduce observed behaviour for the wrong physical reasons \citep{downs2018dynamic,hill2025emulator}. Here, specific parameters representative of restricted subglacial efficiency, are needed to match the periodicity of lake fill--drain cycles. Accordingly, we suggest that meaningful improvement in simulated jökulhlaup dynamics are more likely to follow from incorporating missing physical processes than from parameter optimisation alone.

Notwithstanding these limitations, and recognising that our model successfully reproduces flood timing across a multi-decadal record without tuning to individual events, we view the discrepancy in flood duration as a tractable modelling challenge rather than a fundamental limitation. The framework presented here is readily extensible, and the lake model is parameterised by relatively few, geometrically-interpretable quantities (e.g., lake area, ice thickness at the ice--lake contact, and extraglacial inputs) meaning the model could be straightforwardly applied to other ice-marginal lake systems, including systems with multiple lakes, without modification of the numerical scheme. In addition, our results suggest that periodically draining ice-marginal lakes may represent promising alternative benchmarks for evaluating machine-learning-based emulators of current and future models of subglacial hydrology \citep[e.g.,][]{brinkerhoff2021constraining,verjans2024accelerating,hill2025computationally}. Ice-marginal lakes offer spatially integrated, remotely observable records \citep{domgaard2024altimetry,livingstone2025ice} that are less impacted by the substantial point-to-point variability that characterises the borehole and moulin datasets \citep{murray1995black,rada2018channelized,ing2026connectivity} which have been previously used to evaluate emulators of subglacial hydrology \citep[e.g.,][]{hill2025emulator}.

\section{Summary and conclusions}

We present an ice-marginal lake coupled to a 2D model of subglacial hydrology and ice dynamics, advancing beyond previous 1D models to include lateral flood propagation and coupling between the lake and distributed and channelised drainage components. Our model reproduces characteristic jökulhlaup cycles and patterns of basal sliding, but additionally shows that system-wide pressure changes are important controls on jökulhlaup timing and peak discharge. These water pressure changes, whether driven by basal meltwater availability or system efficiency, alter the 'leakage' rate of the lake, and control whether drainage is routed via high discharge channels or is accommodated within the distributed system. Our hydrology 2D solution enables application to the Isunnguata Sermia glacier in West Greenland, where our model successfully reproduces observed lake level fluctuations over a 17 year record, without tuning to individual events. Predicted jökulhlaup remain unrealistically long, which we attribute to missing physics including: no representation of ice uplift and constant lake water temperatures. Nonetheless, we emphasise that our approach is promising, readily extensible, and applicable to other glacier--lake systems.

\subsection{The Glacier Drainage System model}\label{Appendix:GlaDS}

As described fully in \citet{werder2013modeling}, the hydraulic potential at the bed, $\phi$, is defined at the bed by: 

\begin{equation}
\label{appeq:GlaDSdirBC}
\begin{aligned}
\phi = \phi_{\mathrm{m}} + p_{\mathrm{w}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

with water pressure, $p_{\mathrm{w}}$, and elevation potential

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:elevationpotential}
\begin{aligned}
\phi_{\mathrm{m}} = \rho_{\mathrm{w}}gz_{\mathrm{b}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $\rho_{\mathrm{w}}$ is water density, $g$ is gravitational acceleration and $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ is bed elevation. In turn, effective pressure $N$ is given by: 

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn}
N = p_{\mathrm{i}}-p_{\mathrm{w}} = \phi_{\mathrm{O}}-\phi,
\end{equation}

where $p_{\mathrm{i}}$ is ice overburden pressure ($p_{\mathrm{i}} = \rho_{\mathrm{i}}gH$) and $\phi_{\mathrm{O}}$ is the overburden potential ($\rho_{\mathrm{O}} = \phi_{\mathrm{m}}+p_{\mathrm{i}}$).

In the original GlaDS \citep{werder2013modeling}, sheet discharge, $q_{\mathrm{s}}$ is given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn:gladsqs}
\begin{aligned}
q_{\mathrm{s}} = -k_{\mathrm{s}}h_{\mathrm{s}}^{\alpha}|\nabla \phi|^{\beta}\nabla \phi,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the sheet conductivity; $h_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the sheet thickness; and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are flow exponents. Following the Darcy-Weisbach law, when flow is fully turbulent the flow exponents $\alpha=\mathrm{5/4}$ and $\beta=\mathrm{3/2}$. Here we use the \citet{hill2024improved} laminar--turbulent transition model, and with $\alpha_{\mathrm{s}}= \mathrm{5/4}$ as here, Eq~\ref{appeqn:gladsqs} becomes:  

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: Transition}
\begin{aligned}
q_{\mathrm{s}} = -\frac{\nu}{2 \omega} \left(\frac{h_{\mathrm{b}}}{h_{\mathrm{s}}}\right)^{1 / 2} \hspace{10em}\\
\hspace{3em} \cdot \left(-1 + \sqrt{1 + 4 \frac{\omega}{\nu} \left(\frac{h_{\mathrm{s}}}{h_{\mathrm{b}}}\right)^{1 / 2} k_{\mathrm{s}} h^3 |\nabla \phi|} \right) \frac{\nabla \phi}{|\nabla \phi|}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\omega$ is a laminar-–turbulent transition parameter (corresponding to the Reynolds number at which water becomes fully turbulent), $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the bed bump height, and $v$ is the kinematic viscosity of water at 0$^{\degree}$C. 

%In GlaDS, sheet thickness refers to the thickness of the cavity layer only and there is no representation of viscoelastic deformation of the overlying ice once $p_{\mathrm{w}} > p_{\mathrm{i}}$. Here, following \citeauthor{stevens2022tidewater} (\citeyear{stevens2022tidewater}, based on earlier work by \citeauthor{hewitt2013seasonal} \citeyear{hewitt2013seasonal}), we separate the distributed sheet into elastic sheet thickness $h_{\mathrm{el}}$, and cavity sheet thickness $h_{\mathrm{cav}}$, adding them together to give the overall sheet thickness, $h_{\mathrm{s}} = h_{\mathrm{el}} + h_{\mathrm{cav}}$. The elastic sheet thickness is directly related to the water pressure by:

%\begin{equation}
%\label{appeqn: hs_el}
%\begin{aligned}
%h_{\mathrm{el}}=h_c\left(\frac{p_{\mathrm{w}}}{p_{\mathrm{i}}}\right)^\gamma+h_{\varepsilon 2}\left[-N_{-}+\frac{1}{2} %N_{\varepsilon}\left(1-N_{+} / N_{\varepsilon}\right)_{+}^2\right],
%\end{aligned}
%\end{equation}

%where $h_{\mathrm{c}}$ is the elastic sheet depth scale, $\gamma$, is the elastic sheet exponent, $h_{\varepsilon}$ is the uplift regularisation rate, and $N_{\varepsilon}$ is the regularising pressure for uplift regularisation rate. In addition, $N_{-} = \mathrm{min}(N,0)$ and $N_{+} = \mathrm{max}(N,0)$, with the second term designed to increase rapidly once $N<0$, as a basic representation of hydraulic jacking.

The cavity sheet thickness evolves through time given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: hs_cav}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial h_{\mathrm{cav}}}{\partial t} = w - r,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

for functions $w$ and $r$, which describe the cavity opening and closing rate, respectively \citep{kamb1987glacier,walder1994channelized}. Cavities open at a rate given by basal velocity $U_{\mathrm{b}}$ over basal bumps with height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: basal sliding}
w(h) = 
	\begin{cases}
	U_{\mathrm{b}}({h_{\mathrm{r}} - h_{\mathrm{s}}})/{l_{\mathrm{r}}} & \text{if } h < h_{\mathrm{r}} \\
	0 & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
\end{equation}

where $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ is the horizontal cavity spacing. Viscous ice deformation leads to cavity closure, related to the effective pressure, $N$ by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: viscous closure}
\begin{aligned}
r(h,N) = Ah|N|^{n-1}N, 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $A$ is the rheological constant of ice, multiplied by a first order geometrical factor (h, but double check it is sheet thickness), and $n$ is Glen's flow law exponent. 

Sheet elements exchange water with channels, the cross-sectional of which, $S$, evolve through time due to the dissipation of potential energy, $\Pi$, sensible heat exchange, $\Xi$, and cavity closure rates due to viscous ice creep $v_{\mathrm{c}}$

\begin{equation}
\label{apeq:channel cross section}
\frac{\partial S}{\partial t}=\frac{\Xi-\Pi}{\rho_{\mathrm{i}} L}-v_c,
\end{equation}
\noindent
where $\rho_{\mathrm{i}}$ is the ice density and $L$ is the latent heat of fusion. 

Finally, channel discharge, $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ is also related to the hydraulic potential gradient by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: Qc}
\begin{aligned}
Q_{\mathrm{c}} = -k_{\mathrm{c}}S^{\alpha_{\mathrm{c}}}\left|\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial s}\right|^{\beta_{c}-2}\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial s},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ is the channel conductivity; $s$ is the horizontal coordinate along a channel; and $\alpha_{\mathrm{c}}$/$\beta_{\mathrm{c}}$ are channel flow exponents.
