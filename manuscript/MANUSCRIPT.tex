\begin{abstract}
Ice-marginal lakes are an increasingly common feature of glacierised landscapes, and their sudden drainage beneath glaciers (a jökulhlaup) can threaten downstream communities and infrastructure. Numerous efforts to model jökulhlaups have been made, though typically these models are one-dimensional; representing a single channel connected to a lake, and unable to represent lateral jökulhlaup propagation through the subglacial system as a result. Here, we use a fully coupled model of subglacial hydrology and basal sliding with a time-evolving ice-marginal lake located at its boundary, to simulate jökulhlaups within a 2D subglacial drainage system. In experiments on a synthetic domain, the model produces stable, recurrent jökulhlaup cycles, and glacier acceleration during flood onset followed by abrupt slowdown at peak flood discharge. Sensitivity testing highlights the efficiency of the subglacial hydrology system as a key control on flood timing, peak discharge, and the basal sliding response. We also explore our model's ability to represent an observed record of jökulhlaups by applying it to Isunnguata Sermia, west Greenland. The model successfully reproduces variability over a 17-year period, but underpredicts peak flood discharges, likely because its formulation omits ice uplift and lake temperature variability. These results establish the coupling of a lake to 2D subglacial hydrology and ice dynamics as a viable approach for multi-decadal jökulhlaup simulation, and highlight the need to better constrain subglacial drainage efficiency in jökulhlaup models. 
\end{abstract}

\introduction

Ice-marginal lakes, glacier-abutting water bodies dammed by ice, are increasing in size and frequency as a result of lake basin geometry changes driven by glacier thinning and retreat \citep{clague2012climate,shugar2020rapid,molg2021inventory}. Around the Greenland Ice Sheet, $>$3,300 ice-marginal lakes have been recorded \citep{how2021greenland}. Ice-marginal lakes have a significant influence on ice dynamics \citep{carrivick2022ice,domgaard2024altimetry,harpur2025emerging,harpur2026ice,livingstone2025ice}, and many hold potential to repeatedly and catastrophically drain beneath glaciers in flood events called jökulhlaups \citep{ng2003clague}. These events pose significant risk for downstream communities \citep{mernild2008jokulhlaup}, are increasingly important considerations with respect to hydropower infrastructure \citep{ahlstrom2008evaluation,priebe2024glacial}, and Pleistocene jökulhlaups are considered large enough to have precipitated abrupt changes in global ocean circulation and climate \citep{teller1995history,clark2001freshwater,mangerud2004ice,sufke2022arctic}.

Numerous attempts to realise a numerical jökulhlaup theory have been made \citep[e.g.,][]{nye1976water,spring1981numerical,clarke1982glacier,ng1998mathematical,fowler1999breaking,ng2003clague,kessler2004testing,ng2007climatic,kingslake2013modelling}. \citet{nye1976water} presented the first thermomechanical description, in which an ice-dammed lake drains via a single semi-circular subglacial channel melted upwards into the ice base and which is connected to the glacier terminus. During the first stage of a flood (the `rising limb' of a flood hydrograph) the cross-sectional area of the channel increases as heat dissipated by flowing water drives wall melting. A positive feedback between channel enlargement and the rate of melting causes escalating discharge and rapid lake drainage. As the lake drains, water pressure drops, the rate of channel enlargement falls, and ice deformation leads to channel closure and flood termination (the `falling limb' of a flood hydrograph).

Numerical solution of the Nye model \citep[e.g.,][]{spring1981numerical,clarke1982glacier,ng1998mathematical,fowler1999breaking} highlighted the need for full coupling between the lake and the subglacial drainage system when recreating jökulhlaup hydrographs, i.e., lake depth sets pressure at the channel inlet, and water flux at the channel inlet must equal the lake outflow. However, these studies demonstrated that such coupling produces flood cycles which grow unstably. \citet{fowler1999breaking} established that stable and recurrent flood cycles can occur if the channel receives a uniform meltwater supply, the model includes spatial dependence of the channel variables, and there is a retrograde slope towards the lake (negative basic hydraulic gradient) which allows a seal to develop between floods. \citet{kingslake2013modelling} extended the \citet{fowler1999breaking} model by allowing the flood channel to exchange water with an adjacent cavity drainage system (owing to effective pressure gradients) while also accounting for basal sliding. The \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model showed that bounded flood cycles can occur even in the presence of a basic positive hydraulic gradient, and further characterised the response of ice velocity to changes in effective pressure at different stages of the jökulhlaup cycle. This work has been subsequently extended to include variable extraglacial lake input \citep[e.g., precipitation and snowmelt;][]{kingslake2015chaotic}, systems comprising connected chains of lakes \citep{stubblefield2019modeling}, transitions between distributed and channelised systems at the ice--lake contact \citep{schoof2020analysis} and variable lake basin geometry \citep{jenson2022long}. 

These coupled solutions of jökulhlaups have remained one-dimensional (1D), in the sense that they capture variations along a flood conduit or pathway defined as a line or curve at the ice-bed interface \citep[e.g.,][]{fowler1999breaking,kingslake2013modelling}. Though this level of description permits analysis of some complex dynamics \citep{kingslake2015chaotic,stubblefield2019modeling,schoof2020analysis}, it precludes a representation of lateral jökulhlaup propagation along the subglacial interface \citep{kingslake2013modelling} and cannot describe the interaction of floodwater with the broader subglacial drainage network, ice motion, or basal topography (including where the lake margin meets the subglacial interface) in a spatially-extended manner. 

Meanwhile, the development of spatially-extended continuum models of subglacial hydrology \citep[e.g.,][]{werder2013modeling,sommers2018shakti}, and their coupling to ice dynamics \citep{gilbert2022consistent,cook2022fully,pelle2024subglacial}, has enabled widespread application of 2D models to investigate subglacial drainage processes in real-world domains \citep[e.g.,][]{dow2019role,ehrenfeucht2023seasonal,ehrenfeucht2025Antarctic,narayanan2025simulating}. Enabled by these advancements, here we extend the work of \citet{kingslake2013modelling} to couple jökulhlaup behaviour with the subglacial drainage system and glacier motion, by using a sophisticated 2D subglacial hydrological model coupled to an ice-flow model that accounts for basal sliding, and posing an ice-marginal lake (with evolving water depth) as a boundary condition. We experiment with this model first on a synthetic domain under controlled conditions (Section~\ref{application:synth}), and then apply it to Isunnguata Sermia, an outlet glacier in west Greenland (Section~\ref{application:greenland}). The synthetic tests serve to characterise model sensitivity and establish parameter bounds, while the Greenland application assesses the model's ability to reproduce an observed record of ice-marginal lake fill--drain cycles.

\section{Model formulation}
We simulate subglacial hydrology through multiple jökulhlaup cycles by modifying the 2D \sloppy{Glacier Drainage System} (GlaDS) model \citep{werder2013modeling}, specifically the version of GlaDS as integrated into the Ice-sheet and Sea-level System Model \citep[ISSM; ][ commit a9a3804]{larour2012continental}. For simplicity, the lake is assumed to have a fixed horizontal cross-sectional area (i.e., no hypsometric variation) and lake depth through time which varies according to water flux at the lake/glacier boundary. To represent the evolution of the lake and its influence on ice-dynamics, our model includes coupling between: i) the subglacial hydrological system and the ice-marginal lake, ii) the subglacial hydrological system and ice dynamics, and iii) the ice-marginal lake and ice dynamics (Fig.~\ref{FIG:1}). In Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS} we describe the ISSM-GlaDS model and relevant additional modifications to the version originally presented by \citet{werder2013modeling}. We describe the equations governing ice-marginal lake evolution in Section~\ref{methods:Lake}, and our method of numerical solution in Section~\ref{methods:Numeric}.

\subsection{Ice flow and subglacial drainage models}\label{methods:GlaDS}

GlaDS is a widely used  2D continuum model of subglacial hydrology \citep{dow2019role,  mcarthur2023basal,hayden2023examining,hepburn2024organization,ehrenfeucht2025Antarctic}. It operates on an anisotropic mesh and includes a description of i) distributed flow through a linked-cavity network system represented as a continuous sheet with variable thickness, and ii) channelised flow through Röthlisberger channels that are located along element edges. Sheet elements exchange water with channels, and the cross-sectional area of these channels evolves through time due to the dissipation of potential energy, sensible heat exchange, and cavity closure rates due to viscous ice creep. Discharge in both the distributed ($\bm{q}_{\mathrm{s}}$, $\mathrm{m}^2\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) and channelised system ($Q_{\mathrm{c}}$, $\mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) is driven by gradients in the hydraulic potential, $\nabla \phi$ ($\mathrm{Pa}\,\mathrm{m}^{-1}$), with steeper hydraulic gradients driving enhanced water discharge. In GlaDS, $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ is always non-zero along every internal edge, though few reach a discernible discharge. For ease of visualisation we follow \citet{werder2013modeling} in declaring edges where $Q_{\mathrm{c}} \geq 1\, \mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ to be `meaningful channels' (e.g., Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e). Water in the distributed and channelised drainage components is assumed to exist at the pressure melting point. An advantage of GlaDS for simulating jökulhlaup pathways is that the location of these meaningful channels is automatically determined and may evolve throughout a simulation without prior prescription: a behaviour not represented in previous 1D jökulhlaup models. Boundary conditions are applied in GlaDS either as a prescribed flux (typically for upstream inflow boundaries) or hydraulic potential (for downstream outflow boundaries) in terms of $\phi$. We refer interested readers to Appendix~\ref{Appendix:GlaDS} for an overview of key GlaDS equations, and to \citet{werder2013modeling} for a full description.

ISSM is a finite element higher-order ice-flow model which operates on anisotropic meshes and can be coupled to additional modelling components such as GlaDS \citep{larour2012continental}. In addition to the ice-marginal lake modifications, which we describe below (Section~\ref{methods:Lake}), the primary difference between the original GlaDS \citep{werder2013modeling} and the ISSM version used here \citep{ehrenfeucht2023seasonal} is that rather than assuming water flow within the continuous sheet to be turbulent \citep[cf:][]{werder2013modeling}, we allow it to switch freely between laminar and turbulent flow according to the local Reynolds number, by implementing the \citet{hill2024improved} transition parameterisation. We also prevent cavity expansion by ice creep when effective pressure, $N < 0$ Pa ($N=p_{\mathrm{i}}-p_{\mathrm{w}}$, where $p_{\mathrm{i}}$ is ice pressure and $p_{\mathrm{w}}$ is water pressure) because GlaDS does not include mechanisms to represent uplift of basal ice (separation at the ice-bed interface) once water pressure exceeds ice overburden \citep[e.g.,][]{tsai2010model,schoof2012flotation,dow2015modeling}. Without a physical representation of uplift, allowing cavity expansion by ice creep when $N<0$ Pa is undesirable and has been shown to inhibit channel formation in overpressurised regions of the bed \citep{hill2024improved}.

To capture the influence of ice-marginal lake drainage on basal sliding, we include a two-way coupling between ice flow and subglacial hydrology. The GlaDS-derived effective pressure $N$ modifies basal velocity $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ ($\mathrm{m}\, \mathrm{a}^{-1}$) by governing frictional resistance at the ice-bed interface. In turn, the magnitude of $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ modifies the rate at which cavities open and thereby controls the thickness of the sheet (Eq.~\ref{appeqn: hs_cav}--\ref{appeqn: basal sliding}). These interactions featured in the \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model, but the use of ISSM--GlaDS here enables the resulting ice-dynamical variations to be fully captured in 2D. As in the model of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, we do not account for frictional heating in our solution.

Coupling between ISSM and GlaDS is a relatively new development, and the influence of basal friction parameters remains the subject of ongoing research \citep[e.g.,][]{khan2024inland,McArthur2026impact}, though model response is known to be strongly influenced by the choice of friction law \citep{choi2023impact}. We view this work as necessarily explorative, and do not undertake a full analysis of the modelling interactions between subglacial hydrology and ice dynamics. Rather, we seek to capture the broad influence that ice-marginal lake drainage cycles have upon ice dynamics. Accordingly, we present results using two widely applied friction laws: i) the `Budd-style' friction law \citep{budd1984three} implemented in ISSM in terms of basal stress with the form:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:frictionBudd}
%\begin{aligned}
\tau_{\mathrm{b}} = C^{2}_{\mathrm{b}} N^{\mathrm{r}} U_{\mathrm{b}}^{s} ,
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where $\tau_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the basal stress magnitude (Pa), $C_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the friction coefficient, $N$ is effective pressure, $U_{\mathrm{b}}$ is basal velocity magnitude (the modulus of $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$), and both $r$ and $s$ are friction coefficients, and ii) a Schoof-style friction law \citep{schoof2005effect,gagliardini2007finite} with the form: 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:frictionSchoof}
%\begin{aligned}
\tau_{\mathrm{b}}=\frac{C_{\mathrm{s}}^2 v_{\mathrm{b}}^m}{\left(1+\left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{s}}^2}{C_{\max } N}\right)^{1 / m} U_{\mathrm{b}}\right)^m},
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where $C_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the \textit{Schoof} friction coefficient, $m$ is a friction law exponent, and $C_{\mathrm{max}}$ is Iken's bound. In our experiments on a synthetic domain we use the Budd-style friction law (Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionBudd}, Section~\ref{application:synth}). In the Greenland experiment (Section~\ref{application:greenland}), with an observational constraint on $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$, we use the Schoof-style friction law (Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionSchoof}). Following \citet{mcarthur2023basal}, and \citet{ehrenfeucht2023seasonal}, we limit $N$ to $\geq$3\% of ice overburden pressure with both friction laws to prevent instabilities as $N \to 0$ Pa. 

\subsection{Lake evolution}\label{methods:Lake}

In our model, a lake is defined as a specific Dirichlet boundary condition (see below)  at $n$ vertices along the boundary of the ice-lake contact. 

As in previous studies \citep[e.g.,][]{kingslake2013modelling}, the lake depth, $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ ($\mathrm{m}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:1}), evolves according to the continuity equation:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lakecontinuity}
%\begin{aligned}
\frac{\mathrm{d} l_{\mathrm{h}}}{\mathrm{d} t} = \frac{1}{A_{\mathrm{L}}}[Q_{\mathrm{in}}-Q_{\mathrm{r}}],
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where $A_\mathrm{L}$ is the lake area ($\mathrm{m}^2$), $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ ($\mathrm{m}^3\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) represents extraglacial lake input (e.g., rainfall, surface runoff) and $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ ($\mathrm{m}^3\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$) represents outflow from the lake (when $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is negative there is inflow into the lake). Here, we also include the water exchange between the lake and the sheet \citep[unlike][who isolated cavities from the lake]{kingslake2013modelling}, so that $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sumQr}
%\begin{aligned}
Q_{\mathrm{r}}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( q_{\mathrm{s},i}(t) + Q_{\mathrm{c},i}(t) \right),
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\noindent
or the sum of the normal component of the continuous sheet discharge $\mathbf{q}_{\mathrm{s}}$, integrated along the $i$th boundary edge, $q_{\mathrm{s},i}$  ($\mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$), and channel discharge, $Q_{\mathrm{c},i}$ at the ice-lake contact vertices. As a boundary condition at and between vertices along the ice-lake interface, hydrostatic lake pressure fixes the hydraulic potential, $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ to be: 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lakephi}
%\begin{aligned}
\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}(t) = \rho_{\mathrm{w}}gz_{\mathrm{b}} + \rho_{\mathrm{w}}gl_{\mathrm{h}}(t) ,
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

at all times, where $g$ is gravitational acceleration ($\mathrm{m}\,\mathrm{s}^{-2}$) and $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ is bed elevation ($\mathrm{m \ a.s.l}$). Therefore, lake filling/draining alters $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ and thus $\nabla \phi$ and sheet/channel discharge along the ice-lake contact boundary (see Eq.~\ref{appeqn:gladsqs} \&~\ref{appeqn: Qc}). Between neighbouring vertices which do and do not describe an ice-lake-contact boundary, the value of the Dirichlet boundary condition is interpolated. In the case of an ice--lake contact defined on a single vertex (as in the synthetic experiments described below), the sheet discharge in Eq.~\ref{eq:sumQr} is integrated over the combined half-edge length either side of the ice--lake contact. This maintains consistent dimensions (flux per unit length) and avoids double-counting in assembly. Finally, because GlaDS assumes that subglacial water temperatures are equal to the pressure melting point, we assume that the lake water also exists at the pressure melting point. The consequences of this decision are explored in Section~\ref{discussion:limitations}.

\subsection{Method of numerical solution}\label{methods:Numeric}

At each new timestep, we solve for $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ using a Picard fixed-point iteration scheme comprising an outer and inner loop. In the outer loop, we integrate Eq~\ref{eq:lakecontinuity} forward in time to estimate $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ at the current (i.e., new) timestep given the current estimate of $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (initialised from the previous timestep), using this $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ to set $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$. In the inner loop, GlaDS solves for the domain-wide $\phi$ until it satisfies a set convergence criteria, updating $N$ as it does so \citep[see:][]{werder2013modeling,hill2024improved}. Following the inner loop converging, and given the estimated field of $\phi$ ($X,Y$), the model updates $h_{\mathrm{s}}$, $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$, and $q_{\mathrm{s}}$, before summing $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ and $q_{\mathrm{s}}$ at lake boundary nodes to give a new estimate of $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Eqn.~\ref{eq:sumQr}). The outer loop iterates until the maximum pointwise change in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ between successive iterations falls below a prescribed absolute tolerance.


\section{Model application to a synthetic domain}\label{application:synth}

First, we conduct synthetic simulations with the model to establish its consistency with \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, and to further develop their work. We use a 10$\times$3 km rectangular domain (Figure~\ref{FIG:2}c--f), discretised on a 2D triangular mesh with a characteristic edge length of 100 m ($n_{\mathrm{vertices}}$=2486). The bed elevation, $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ is characterised by a gentle slope ($\sin=0.0075$) dipping towards the terminus, and ice thickness follows a parabolic profile that increases from $H=50$ m at $X=0$ km to $H\approx155$ m at $X = 10$ km. The simulations here are aimed at facilitating comparison with those of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, so we use a similar domain as theirs, which was chosen to be loosely analogous to the Merzbacher Lake and South Inylchek Glacier, Kyrgyzstan \citep{ng2009temporal}. An ice-marginal lake with $A_{\mathrm{L}}=5\ \mathrm{km}^{2$ is connected to the glacier at a single upglacier boundary vertex $(X, Y) = (10,\ 1.5\ \text{km}$), isolating the rest of the lake margin from the subglacial drainage system. This is necessary because \citet{kingslake2013modelling} coupled the lake to a single flood channel only and did not couple the adjacent linked cavity system to the lake. 

Model variables are given in Table~\ref{tab:Var} and our synthetic model parameters are given in Table~\ref{tab:Synth}. The parameter dependency of GlaDS has been explored extensively \citep[e.g.,][]{dow2016modeling,dow2020totten,hill2024improved}. We limit our sensitivity experiments in Section~\ref{ss:sensitivity} below to parameters regarded as critical to subglacial drainage and known to be the largest sources of uncertainty in subglacial hydrology models \citep{werder2013modeling,hill2025computationally,hill2025emulator} which include: i) the channel and sheet conductivity terms $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ and $k_{\mathrm{s}}$; ii) the basal bump height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$; iii) the channel sheet width, $l_{\mathrm{c}}$; and iv) the cavity spacing, $l_{\mathrm{r}}$; as well as the terms which control the input of water into both the subglacial system: v) basal melt rate $M_{\mathrm{r}}$; and vi) the lake refill rate, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$, which is held constant through time in the synthetic experiments. A reference simulation is conducted at the mid-range values of these parameters as listed in Table~\ref{tab:Synth}. Then, as is common practice \citep{dow2022role}, we vary one parameter at a time between a minimum and maximum bound (Table~\ref{tab:Synth}; informed both by previous work and the limits of numerical stability), in additional runs (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}). 

Each simulation is run from $t=0$ to 15 years at 30-minute timesteps. We use a Budd-style friction law (Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionBudd}) with $C_{\mathrm{b}} = 100\ \mathrm{kg}^{3/8}\,\mathrm{m}^{-1/2}\,\mathrm{s}^{-5/8}$, $r = 1/4$, and $s = 1/4$ \citep[chosen to be consistent with][]{bindschadler1983importance,kingslake2013modelling}. The ice dynamic model is initialised with $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}} = (-100,0)\ \mathrm{m,a}^{-1}$, with Dirichlet boundary conditions ($\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}} = (0,0)\ \mathrm{m\,a}^{-1}$) at the glacier terminus and stress-free Neumann boundary conditions elsewhere. The hydrology model is initialised with $\phi$ equivalent to 90\% of ice overburden pressure, and an initial sheet thickness, $h_{\mathrm{s}}$ equal to half the bump height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$. We prescribe $\phi$ at the terminus to be $\approx$1$\times10^{4}\ \mathrm{Pa}$ as our GlaDS Dirichlet boundary condition. Though arbitrary, this does not qualitatively affect our results below. We impose zero water inflow as our Neumann boundary condition at the other three boundaries. The ice-marginal lake has an initial water height of 30 m ($l_{\mathrm{h}}$ at $t=0$). The evolution of the reference case through time is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}, and the sensitivity of lake height ($l_{\mathrm{h}}$), flood discharge ($Q_{\mathrm{r}}$), and basal velocity magnitude ($U_{\mathrm{b}}$) through time is shown in Figs.~\ref{FIG:3} \&~\ref{FIG:4}.

\subsection{Stable flood cycles}

In the reference simulation, the lake undergoes recurrent, stable, and asymmetric fill-drain cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}a). Drainage is associated with a recurring flood with stable maximum amplitude and duration (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}b). The lake only partially drains in each flood cycle, and never reaches a height necessary to sustain flotation ($N=0$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}f). In the initial 5 years of transient evolution, when the model adjusts to initial conditions, peak flood discharge increases with each successive flood, and the lake reaches successively lower post-flood lowstands (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}a,b). Thereafter, the flood cycles reach a stable periodicity and clockwise hysteresis (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}f) with flood cycles occurring approximately every 1.5 years. As will be discussed in Section~\ref{discussion}, these simulated cycles are comparable to---yet with notable differences from---those of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}.

The evolution of the subglacial channel system through a single flood cycle is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e (an animated version is shown in \hyperref[Apfig:SuppMovie]{Movie A1}), and the width-averaged basal velocity response over the same flood cycle and into the following is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g. At the start of a flood cycle (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c), as the lake approaches its highstand, channelised drainage is limited and $\nabla\phi$ is generally perpendicular to the ice surface slope. Close to the ice--lake contact ($X > \mathrm{9\ km}$), contours of hydraulic potential form ridges pointing downglacier from the ice--lake contact. In GlaDS, sheet discharge, $q_{\mathrm{s}}$, is directed perpendicular to these contours of $\nabla\phi$ and such ridges would induce sheet flow out of the lake. Four short ($<$100 m), low-discharge ($\geq$1 m$^{3}\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}$) channels remain open following formation during the previous flood cycle, three at the terminus and one at the ice--lake contact.

During the lake filling phase, increasing $l_\mathrm{{h}}$ drives $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ up (Eq.~\ref{eq:lakephi}), resulting in a steeper $\nabla \phi$ across the domain and promoting channelised drainage close to the ice--lake contact (\hyperref[Apfig:SuppMovie]{Movie A1}). During the lake drainage phase, initiated once $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ begins to rapidly exceed the lake refill rate, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ (when $l_\mathrm{{h}} \approx \mathrm{75\ m}$ and $t \approx \mathrm{7.9 \, yrs}$), an arborescent channel structure originating from the lake extends across the full $Y-$axis extent of the domain, perpendicular to the contours of $\phi$, and deviating from the direction of ice-surface slope. Simultaneously, channels extend upglacier from the terminus, and contours of $\phi$ become increasingly convex downglacier. At the flood peak, channel tributaries coalesce into the three primary terminus channels which evacuate water from the system. As the lake level drops sharply during a flood, $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ drops, reducing $\nabla \phi$, and in turn reducing $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ below the level necessary to sustain channels across the domain, including at the ice--lake contact. Once $Q_{\mathrm{r}} < Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ the flood terminates and the cycle begins again. Each cycle takes $\approx$1.5 years with the rising and falling limb of the flood lasting $\approx$6 months. 

Basal velocity, $U_{\mathrm{b}}$ changes significantly during a flood cycle (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g), tracking the development of a significant channelised drainage system. The glacier accelerates during the year-long flood initiation phase before channelised drainage is widely established, then decelerates abruptly (in les than two months) as channels become well-established, $\approx$two months before the flood peak (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g). Ice flow acceleration propagates from the ice--lake contact towards the terminus, with maximum velocity sustained within 2 km of the ice--lake contact.

\subsection{Parameter sensitivity}\label{ss:sensitivity}


Fig.~\ref{FIG:3} plots the time series of lake height $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ and lake discharge $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ from a total of 14 simulations across the maximum (red lines) and minimum (blue lines) parameter values, with the reference `\textit{mid-point}' simulation in grey. The majority of simulations yield stable and recurring asymmetric lake filling and drainage cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[i]--f[i]) with corresponding variations in $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ at the ice--lake contact (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[ii]--f[ii]). The parameter variations most strongly influence the amplitude of lake height and its rate of change, both of which influence the timing of flood initiation and peak flood discharge, with higher amplitude lake depth cycles leading to higher peak flood discharges \citep{ng2003clague}.

Increasing channel conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, in effect making channels more sensitive to $\nabla \phi$ (see Eq.~\ref{appeqn: Qc}), leads to lower lake highstands as the lake drains sooner. However, peak discharge is higher as the lake also drains more quickly and drains entirely. At maximum $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, there is a longer transient before the model reaches a fixed flood recurrence interval, draining at a higher frequency than the default value of $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ until $\sim 11 \ \mathrm{years}$ into the simulation, at which point flood cycles occur at approximately the same interval as in the default case (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[ii]). Conversely, at minimum $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, the capacity of channels to drain the lake is inhibited and flood onset is delayed. Because channels grow slowly, peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ is also dampened, leading to relatively long flood recurrence intervals ($\sim 4\ \mathrm{years}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}a[ii]).

Increasing the sheet conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ increases the capacity of the sheet to accommodate water and delays transmission to channels. This results in reduced lake highstands and delayed flood initiation as the onset of classical channel growth is delayed. Reducing $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ increases the relative efficiency of channels and leads to more vigorous lake-fill drain cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}b[i]), with shorter and higher discharge floods as a result (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}b[ii]). Changing $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ has a similar effect to reducing $k_{\mathrm{s}}$, although at the maximum bump height the sheet becomes so efficient that it can accommodate all lake discharge and within 1 year the lake reaches a stable equilibrium where $Q_{\mathrm{r}} = Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}b[i]). 

Changing the contributing width of the sheet to the channel ($l_{\mathrm{c}}$) or the representative horizontal spacing of cavities ($l_{\mathrm{r}}$) alters the relative efficiency of channelised and sheet drainage. Reducing $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ and increasing $l_{\mathrm{c}}$ reduces the lake highstand and lowers peak discharge, and vice versa when increasing $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ and reducing $l_{\mathrm{c}}$. Finally, increasing the input of water into either the glacier via basal melt rate, $M_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}f) or lake via $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}g) enhances the amplitude of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ oscillations and in turn, peak flood discharge (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}f). 

In all simulations, the maximum lake height remains below the maximum ice thickness (150 m) and does not surpass the effective pressure necessary to initiate ice flotation ($N=0$). In most simulations the lake does not reach a lowstand below $\sim$10 m, only draining completely when $k_{\mathrm{c}} = 0.2 \ \mathrm{m}^{3/2}\, \mathrm{kg}^{-1/2}$, and when $h_{\mathrm{b}}=0.05\ \mathrm{m}$.

\section{Model application to Isunnguata Sermia, Greenland}\label{application:greenland}

Isunnguata Sermia is a land-terminating outlet glacier draining a catchment of $\approx\!16,000\ \mathrm{km}^2$ on the western margin on the Greenland Ice Sheet (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}a--c). Its terminus region is characterised by a deep, flow-parallel bedrock trough with an anabranching structure in its upper reaches (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). The glacier terminates into a sub-aerial proglacial river system. A recently described  $\approx\!3\ \mathrm{km}^2$, $\approx\!100\ \mathrm{m}$ deep ice-dammed lake \citep{livingstone2025ice} abuts the northern margin of Isunnguata Sermia's tongue. The lake drains subglacially with a periodicity of 1--3 years, having undergone at least 12 fill-drain cycles between 1987--2024 \citep[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g]{livingstone2025ice}. There are two ice-contact boundaries between Isunnguata Sermia and the ice-marginal lake, a prominent ice cliff boundary along the western margin of the lake through which the lake drains, and an intermittent boundary along the eastern margin that receives drainage from the glacier through an emerging subglacial stream during lake lowstands, and becomes ice-contact at lake highstands \citep{livingstone2025ice}.

\subsection{Model setup} \label{application:setup}

We discretise our Isunnguata Sermia domain \citep[derived from][]{lindback2015subglacial} using an anistropic mesh with an edge length of 150 m close to terminus, increasing to 2 km close to the upper margin of our domain at the approximate ELA (at \( H \approx 1600\ \text{m} \)). The mesh is further refined to a typical edge length of 50 m close to the ice-marginal lake (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). Bedmachine v5 is used for $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ and $H$ \citep[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}b,c]{morlighem2017bedmachine,Morlighem_Et_Al_2022}. Our domain is clipped to exclude areas of the glacier where $H<100 \, \mathrm{m}$ to ensure numerical stability. Basal velocity, $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$, is initialised from the 2021 MEaSUREs annual surface velocity mosaic \citep{MEASURES}. Geothermal heat flux is taken from \citet{shapiro2004inferring}, and basal melt rates are taken from \citet{karlsson2021first}. The flow-law rate factor $A$ within the ice-column is derived using surface ice temperatures from \citet{ettema2009higher} and assuming a temperature-dependent Arrhenius relation following \citet{cuffey2010physics}. 

Our model includes multiple boundary conditions in order to represent Isunnguata Sermia. For the hydrological solution, we impose two distinct Dirichlet boundary conditions at three locations: i) at the two ice--lake contact boundaries $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ is allowed to evolve with $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ (see Eq.~\ref{eq:lakephi}) and ii) a fixed atmospheric boundary is imposed at the glacier terminus. At all other vertices, we impose Neumann (zero-inflow) boundary conditions. The ice--lake contacts are manually delineated: seven vertices were identified at the western lake boundary and at the eastern margin we identified the lowest elevation vertex within a group of vertices close to the observed stream. At all ice-lake contact vertices we fix bed elevation equal to the minimum lake elevation (257.9 m a.s.l). Because our model assumes a fixed lake area ($A_{\mathrm{L}}=3\ \mathrm{km}^2$) and cannot dynamically represent intermittent lake-glacier contact along the eastern margin, prescribing the same boundary conditions at the eastern ice--lake contact as along the western ice--lake contact provides a simple and consistent boundary treatment and ensures water can exchange between the subglacial system and the lake at both places. At the glacier terminus we set the Dirichlet boundary condition $\phi$ equal to atmospheric pressure at the lowest elevation boundary vertex (cyan triangle in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). Within the ice-dynamic solution, the boundary condition velocity is given by the initialisation $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ (Dirichlet), except at the western ice--lake contact where velocity is allowed to evolve freely (Neumann). 

Model simulation necessitates knowledge of the spatial field of basal friction coefficient, $C$, which we assume to be time-invariant. Because we have an observational constraint on ice-surface velocity, we use a Schoof-style friction law (see Eq.~\ref{eq:frictionSchoof}, Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}), inverting for the basal friction coefficient using an L-curve scheme \citep[following][]{wolovick2023regularization}. A Schoof-style friction law has been shown to more faithfully represent ice-dynamics with a GlaDS derived $N$ than the Budd-style friction law \citep{mcarthur2023basal}. We run an initial inversion for $C$ using an empirically-derived $N$ (assuming $N=p_{i}$), before running a simple GlaDS-only model run over 10 years, which does not include an ice-marginal lake, surface melt or two-way coupling to ice dynamics. This allows us to derive a more realistic and spatially variable estimated field of $N$ from which a second inversion for basal friction can be obtained. Such an approach has previously been demonstrated to yield more faithful basal inversions in a coupled hydrology model \citep[see][]{ehrenfeucht2023seasonal,mcarthur2023basal}. 

As climate forcing, we use the 10 km resolution daily Modèle Atmosphérique Régional (MAR, v3.14) runoff and accumulation data \citep{fettweis2017reconstructions} between 2007--2025 inclusive. Runoff is routed directly to the bed at all vertices. Whereas we keep $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ fixed in the synthetic experiments (Section~\ref{application:synth}), here the extraglacial water input to the lake is likely to vary with time as a function of air temperature \citep{ng2007climatic,ng2009temporal}. To account for this, we use a simple temperature index \citep[as in][]{kingslake2015chaotic}, where $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ is given by: 


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Tempindex}
%\begin{aligned}
Q_{\mathrm{in}} =
\begin{cases}
\mathrm{max}(kT,1), & T > 0 \\
1, & T \le 0,
\end{cases}
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\noindent
where the temperature-index coefficient $ k $ is equal to $2\ \mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}\, \mathrm{K}^{-1}$. We set $Q_{\mathrm{in}} \geq 1 \ \mathrm{m}^{3}\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ to capture positive changes in lake elevation observed between melt seasons in Fig~\ref{FIG:5}g. 

Since our objective is to assess the model's ability to reproduce the observed record of fill-drain cycles of an ice-marginal lake in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g, we focus on achieving a simulation that yields the closest quantitative match. Guided by insights from the synthetic experiments (Section~\ref{application:synth}), we choose the final parameters (Table~\ref{tab:Greenland}) as follows: a decrease in channel conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{c}}$, to 0.02 $\mathrm{m}^{\frac{3}{2}}\, \mathrm{kg}^{-\frac{1}{2}}$, an increase in sheet conductivity, $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ to 0.05 $\mathrm{Pa\, s}^{-1}$, a decrease in the basal bump height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ to 0.05 m, and changes to both the channel sheet width, $l_{\mathrm{c}}$, and cavity spacing, $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ to 10 m. Compared to the synthetic case, we also increase the englacial void ratio, $e_{\mathrm{v}}$, to 0.01. This has little influence on our synthetic case when tested to the same limits. However, in our Greenland experiments, increasing $e_{\mathrm{v}}$ dampens the model sensitivity to sudden changes in meltwater input to the system and prevents unreasonably high water pressures ($>300\%$ of overburden pressure) in isolated pockets close to the domain boundary. Across sensitivity tests, parameter variations produce changes in the frequency, timing, and amplitude of modelled outputs without altering the qualitative drainage system behaviour.

We simulate the Greenland model forward in time from 1987 to 2025 (for 38 years at a 5 minute timestep). This simulation consists of three phases, the first of which involves running the model (for twenty years of model time) to reach a winter steady state where the 95\textsuperscript{th} percentile difference in sheet thickness between successive timesteps is below $1\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m}$. During the steady state phase, we allow the ice-marginal lake to freely evolve from an initial height of $l_{\mathrm{h}}=70\ \mathrm{m}$, the mean lake height between 1987--2024 \citep{livingstone2025ice}. The steady state phase allows the model to reach a condition approximating the anticipated wintertime hydrological conditions in which there is limited channel activity and water pressures across the domain are close to the flotation fraction ($p_{\mathrm{w}}\approx 0.7\text{--}0.9 \, p_{\mathrm{i}}$). We then run a `spin-up' phase from 1997--2007 where we introduce a climate forcing by gradually increasing the 2007 runoff by 10\% each year. Following this spin-up, we run an unconstrained phase from 2007--2025 where we apply the full climate forcing and compare results with the 2007--2025 lake level record.

\subsection{Flood cycles in Greenland} \label{application:results}

The combined spin-up and unconstrained modelling phase is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}d--g. The spin-up phase (dashed line in Fig~\ref{FIG:5}d--f) is characterised by three cycles of lake filling and partial drainage, each reaching successively higher lake highstands and having a shorter flood recurrence period than the previous one; these cycles track the development of a catchment-wide subglacial drainage system induced by successively more intense melt summers (Fig~\ref{FIG:5}d). 

From 2007 onwards, once the model is subject to the full extent of annual melt-forcing, the lake repeatedly fills and drains with a recurrence interval between floods of $\sim$2--4 years. At the peak of a flood, using model year 2022 as an example, the simulated drainage system of Isunnguata Sermia is characterised by a channel that occupies the central trough and extends $>$45 km upglacier (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c). The majority of surface melt is routed through this `primary drainage axis' towards the glacier terminus, and an anabranching tributary system is visible $\sim$20 km upstream. Near the terminus, a second drainage axis, comprising a high discharge central channel and two lower discharge subsidiary channels, connects the western ice--lake contact to the primary drainage axis, converging $\sim$ 2 km from the terminus.

The lake fill--drain cycles are characterised by slow-rising limbs (between jökulhlaupw) and abrupt drawdowns (jökulhlaups), though their hydrograph shape is irregular. Lake drainages typically occur at the end of a melt season. Lake levels then remain low during the subsequent winter, before filling in an irregular sawtooth fashion \citep[as in][]{ng2009temporal}, with the lake filling more rapidly during summer melt seasons and remaining stable, or lowering slightly, during the intervening winter period. Lake drainage is triggered following the second or third melt season, and the cycle begins anew. 

Each modelled jökulhlaup lasts 3--4 months, far exceeding the typical flood duration of 5--8 days observed at Isunnguata Sermia \citep{livingstone2025ice}. Correspondingly, our modelled peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}\approx$100--200~m$^{3}$~s$^{-1}$ are markedly lower than that of the 2022 jökulhlaup described by \citet{livingstone2025ice}, which had an estimated minimum mean discharge of 480~m$^{3}$~s$^{-1}$ and a likely considerably higher instantaneous peak.

The timing and water height of modelled lake highstands in our simulation phase align well with those in the observed record, capturing variability in both flood timing and the proportion of lake drainage. Eight lake highstands are evident in the observed record (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g) between 2007--2024, seven of which correspond with a simulated lake highstand to within $\sim$2 months and $\pm$ 20 m (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f). Further, there is good overall agreement in the lake lowstand heights, with the model matching observations of partial (e.g., 2010--2011) and complete lake drainage events (e.g., 2007--2008). This match is not perfect (e.g., 2013) and may be influenced by gaps in the observational record \citep{livingstone2025ice}. 

The evolution of channels during a typical jökulhlaup cycle (2019--2023) is shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}. On reaching lake highstand in 2019, the development of a significant channel connection between the lake and the primary drainage axis initiates a jökulhlaup and the drop in lake level (Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}b). As $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ and $\nabla\phi$ lowers at the western ice--lake contact, the lake draining channel is starved of supply and shuts down by creep closure. At the lake lowstand, once $\phi_{\mathrm{contact}}$ is close to atmospheric pressure, drainage into the lake from the eastern ice--lake contact begins in summer 2020 (Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}c) and the lake quickly fills to $\sim 50\ \mathrm{m}$. In the 2021 summer (Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}d) and 2022 summer, no significant channels develop upglacier of the eastern ice--lake contact, and $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ accounts for the rise in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$. At the end of the 2022 melt season (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}, \& Fig.~\ref{FIG:6}e), a relatively high discharge jökulhlaup drains the lake via a series of channels at the wester ice--lake contact and connected to the primary drainage axis. Though seven vertices along the western ice--lake contact are connected to the lake, discharge at the lake boundary is concentrated within a single high-discharge channel. Midway along the flood path, $\approx$2.5 km from the lake, this channel diverges and branches into multiple lower discharge channels running sub-parallel to the main lake-draining channel and perpendicular to $\nabla \phi$, a behaviour also evident in the synthetic runs (see Section~\ref{application:synth} \& Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e). 

Turning to the simulated basal velocity field, $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}=u_{\mathrm{b}},v_{\mathrm{b}}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:7} shows the mean components of $u_{\mathrm{b}}$ and $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ between 2014--2025 and the anomalies, generated by subtracting the overall mean velocity from the mean velocity during a jökulhlaup, associated with the modelled 2022 flood (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}a--d) and the observed 2023 flood (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}e--h) from \citet{livingstone2025ice}. Each velocity field is interpolated onto the same model mesh. The $x$- ($u_{\mathrm{b}}$) and $y$- ($v_{\mathrm{b}}$) components of modelled basal velocity show a mean value (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}a,b) that agrees closely with the mean observed velocity (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}e,f), although our model predicts a higher mean $v_{\mathrm{b}} > 50~\mathrm{m\,a^{-1}}$ close to the lake margin (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}b) than the $v_{\mathrm{b}} \approx 25~\mathrm{m\,a^{-1}}$ observed \citep[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}f]{livingstone2025ice}. The modelled and observed lake-drainage anomalies (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}c,d,g,h) agree well in terms of the overall pattern of slow-down and acceleration, but differ in detail and magnitude. In our model, changes in $u_{\mathrm{b}}$ and $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ during a jökulhlaup are lower in magnitude, and are more spatially restricted than observed (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}). During a modelled jökulhlaup, $u_{\mathrm{b}}$ decreases (corresponding to a westward acceleration) by $20~\mathrm{m\,a^{-1}}$ upglacier of the lake, and $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ increases (corresponding to a northward acceleration) by ${\approx}15~\mathrm{m\,a^{-1}}$, with the acceleration restricted to close to the lake boundary (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}d). In contrast, the observations indicate a more widespread westward acceleration across the full extent of the glacier tongue during a jökulhlaup, with $u_{\mathrm{b}}$ decreasing by ${>}100~\mathrm{m\,a^{-1}}$, and a northward component where $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ increases by ${>}100~\mathrm{m\,a^{-1}}$ towards the lake \citep[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}h]{livingstone2025ice}. The observed $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ is also more spatially variable than the equivalent model output, with ice accelerating towards the ice-marginal lake across a more extensive portion of the lake-adjacent glacier. Observations additionally identify a southward acceleration in $v_{\mathrm{b}}$ close to the glacier terminus that is not reproduced in our model.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

The model presented here, which couples a time-evolving ice-marginal lake to a 2D representation of subglacial hydrology and basal sliding for the first time, corroborates many of the findings of \citet{kingslake2013modelling} from their 1D model. The lake gradually fills at a rate governed by the magnitude of the extraglacial input rate $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$ and jökulhlaups initiate once lake depth $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ reaches between 50--80$\%$ of ice thickness $H$ corresponding to between 55--90$\%$ of ice overburden pressure. The characteristic stable and recurrent jökulhlaup cycles are pervasive across both the reference case (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}) and the majority of sensitivity tests (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}). Similarly, our model produces an overall response in basal velocity, $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$, to flood propagation resembling that of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}. Ice near to the lake accelerates markedly during flood growth and abruptly slows down during flood recession after peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}g \&~\ref{FIG:4}). Such a velocity pattern has been observed during jökulhlaups at Gornergletscher, Switzerland \citep{sugiyama2007reversal} and Skaftá, Iceland \citep{magnusson2007impact,einarsson2016spectrum,einarsson2017subglacial}. 

\subsection{Comparison to previous work}
The overall agreement between the synthetic results in this work and that of earlier work \citep{kingslake2013modelling} is unsurprising; the lakes are governed by closely related equations and the underlying hydrology models assume broadly similar physics. However, our inclusion of a spatially variable (2D) hydrology solution, and our decision to permit hydraulic connectivity between the lake and the adjacent linked cavities, do present behaviours not evident or possible to explore with the 1D \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model. In their model, a single channel is connected to an ice-marginal lake. The channel itself is connected to a linked-cavity system with longitudinal (but not transverse) hydrological variations, and the two drainage components maintain independent effective pressure. Water exchange between the drainage elements occurs at a rate proportional to the pressure differential \citep[as in][]{hewitt2008seasonal}. Crucially, in their model, the lake is hydraulically isolated from the linked cavity system. 

In our model, with subglacial hydrology resolved in two dimensions, pressure continuity is maintained across all channel margins (i.e., at all vertices where channels contact their adjacent sheet); water exchange occurs at the rate necessary to maintain this condition and it is $\nabla \phi$ across neighbouring drainage elements that drives overall water flow. At the ice--lake contact, where $\phi_{contact}$ evolves through time (Eq.~\ref{eq:lakephi}), we maintain pressure continuity by allowing the linked cavities to exchange water with the lake, with sheet discharge integrated over an edge length centred on the single ice--lake contact (see Section~\ref{methods:Lake}). We consider our assumption of water exchange between the continuous sheet and the lake to be more physically plausible than the isolated cavity assumption of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}. This assumption has been shown to match well to observations of jökulhlaups elsewhere, when flux is prescribed across the ice--lake contact \citep[e.g.,][]{flowers2004coupled}.

The clearest distinction between our model and that of \citet{kingslake2013modelling}, is in the different response to changes in basal melt, $M_{\mathrm{r}}$ \citep[denoted by cavity water supply, $M_{\mathrm{c}}$, in ][]{kingslake2013modelling}. In their model, an increase in basal melt reduces the peak flood $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$, whereas here, increasing the basal melt rate (substantially, from a base of 1 m a$^{-1}$ to 5 m a$^{-1}$) nearly doubles peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ from $\approx50\ \mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ to $\approx90\ \mathrm{m}^{3}\, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$  (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}f[ii]). In the \citet{kingslake2013modelling} model, higher basal melt lowers the effective pressure in the linked cavities, accelerates cavity--channel exchanges, promotes earlier channel growth and triggers lake drainage at lower lake levels, with a lower peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$. In our model, with a lake connected to the continuous sheet, elevated basal melt lowers the system-wide effective pressure, reduces $\nabla \phi$ at the ice--lake contact, inhibits lake leakage into the glacier and allows the lake to both fill more quickly and reach a higher $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ before drainage. Once triggered, the greater water supply sustains enlarged channels with a higher peak $Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ that drains the lake to a lower $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ compared to the reference basal melt rate case. This effect in our model is asymmetric; reducing $M_{\mathrm{r}}$ below the baseline has negligible effect on flood recurrence interval or peak discharge (Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}f[ii]), suggesting that below a threshold basal melt rate the glacier-wide effective pressure is governed primarily by the lake drainage cycle itself rather than by the ambient melt supply, and the effective pressure (and in turn basal sliding) becomes effectively decoupled from meltwater availability at the bed. Our results indicate that in glacier--lake systems characterised by relatively low basal meltwater availability, lake dynamics may become the dominant control on glacier sliding velocity over time.

Our results highlight drainage efficiency as a key control on jökulhlaup characteristics and basal sliding response. In our synthetic experiments, the lake drains at a lower highstand to a higher lowstand, with a longer, less intense flood if the efficiency of the distributed sheet is raised relative to the channelised system; for example, by raising the sheet conductivity ($k_{\mathrm{s}}$ Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}), raising the basal bump height ($h_{\mathrm{b}}$ Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}), or decreasing cavity spacing ($l_{\mathrm{r}}$, Fig.~\ref{FIG:3}e). Longer duration, less intense jökulhlaups drive higher and more sustained increases in $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$. With a longer rising limb, greater subglacial discharge is accommodated within the sheet, sustaining the low effective pressure necessary to enable fast ice flow. Conversely, when channelised drainage is relatively efficient (e.g., Fig.~\ref{FIG:4}a,d,f), jökulhlaups are shorter in duration and peak drainage is higher. In turn, the $\mathbf{U}_{\mathrm{b}}$ response is shorter and lower in peak velocity because efficient drainage develops more easily, increasing effective pressure over wide-tracts of the glacier bed and inhibiting basal sliding. The parameters which control drainage efficiency in GlaDS and other comparable hydrology models are known to be highly uncertain \citep{werder2013modeling}. Our results further highlight the drainage system efficiency as a key uncertainty requiring improved observational constraint.

\subsection{2D jökulhlaup propagation}

Subglacial flood channel locations are not predefined in our model; they emerge as part of the solution (Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}). In the synthetic case, the local control of the lake on $\nabla \phi$ generates channels offset by 20--40$^{\degree}$ relative to the central axis of the glacier (Fig.~\ref{FIG:2}c--e). This lateral expansion of the jökulhlaup through the subglacial system results in a distinctive pattern of $\nabla\phi$ and an arborescent channel propagation which expands outwards from the central ice--lake contact position during a flood before collapsing into a few flow-parallel channels close to the glacier terminus. This behaviour contrasts sharply with the ice-flow parallel channels expected in the absence of significant lateral topographic variation whereby the basic hydraulic potential is governed by ice surface slope alone \citep[e.g., see Figure 2 in][]{werder2013modeling}. Lateral propagation of flood water within the subglacial system during a jökulhlaup has been invoked as the cause of short-term lateral deviations in GPS measurements towards the ice-margins during jökulhlaups at Gornergletscher, Switzerland \citep{sugiyama2007reversal}.

Coupling ice-marginal lake evolution to a 2D description of subglacial hydrology is an essential advance over previous work, because the resulting model can be applied to specific glaciers to study jökulhlaup behaviour in a realistic manner. Model application to Isunnguata Sermia (Section~\ref{application:greenland}), shows that the evolution of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ through time in our model (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f) closely matches the observed record reported by \citet[][Fig.~\ref{FIG:5},g]{livingstone2025ice}. Between 2007 and mid 2024, eight significant floods from the ice-marginal lake are recorded from remote sensing records (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g). Of these floods, seven are hindcasted by our model (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f), closely agreeing with observations in terms of timing and the amplitude of change in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$. 

Variability in the timing and character of jökulhlaups in our Isunnguata Sermia experiment is a direct consequence of coupling a lake to a 2D subglacial drainage system and forcing the system with realistic, time-varying climate record (see Section~\ref{application:greenland}). Climate variability through time modulates the system-wide $\nabla \phi$ (and also changes lake input, $Q_{\mathrm{in}}$, see Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}d), thereby reshaping the effective pressure field and the local capacity of the drainage system to transmit lake water. In lower-order models, similar variability in flood discharge arises when a transient climate is imposed \citep{kingslake2015chaotic, ng2009temporal}. Climate input variability propagates through the subglacial drainage system to produce nonlinear variability in jökulhlaup timing and peak discharge (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}e--f). %The lack of such variability in jökulhlaup with the consistent forcing of our synthetic experiments, combined with the close correspondence between modelled and observed lake behaviour at Isunnguata Sermia, suggests that this variability is an emergent feature of our coupling of a lake to a 2D subglacial system, rather than a model artefact. 

As well as a close agreement in terms of $l_{\mathrm{h}}$, our results corroborate additional observations from Isunnguata Sermia. Recently, a series of elevation anomalies have been identified close to the glacier terminus that are interpreted to have been caused by the filling and draining of subglacial lakes \citep{livingstone2019brief,livingstone2025ice}. Three subglacial lakes \citep[labelled SGL 1--3 in][]{livingstone2025ice} lie due south of the primary drainage axis \citep[Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c, \&~\ref{FIG:6}b--e][]{livingstone2019brief} and a fourth, more recently identified \citep[labelled Anomaly 1 in][]{livingstone2025ice}, lies to the southwest of the ice-marginal lake (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c, \&~\ref{FIG:6}b--e). Between June and September 2019, Anomaly 1 experienced rapid subsidence (up to 38 m) after a long period of slow uplift; a pattern consistent with the fill--drain cycles of active subglacial lakes elsewhere \citep{livingstone2025ice}. The sudden subsidence of Anomaly 1 was coincident with the complete drainage of the ice-marginal lake during 2019 (see Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g), and the jökulhlaup is thought to have triggered the release of water along the subglacial flood-path \citep{livingstone2025ice}. In our model, the predicted jökulhlaup flowpath passes directly through Anomaly 1 (as shown in Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}c \&~\ref{FIG:6}b--e) supporting the hypothesis that ice-marginal lake drainage is a triggering mechanism for the sudden drainage of subglacial lakes. 

Finally, our model results reproduce some of the observations relating to ice-dynamics at Isunnguata Sermia. Both our model and observations show acceleration along the primary flow direction during a flood and a component of acceleration towards the lake concentrated along the western ice--lake contact (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}). \citet{livingstone2025ice} attributed the observed acceleration to i) ice inflow into the lake as a result of a sudden drop in water level and change in force balance at the ice front, and ii) a reduction in basal friction as the sudden influx of flood water overpressurises remaining channels and the nascent distributed system becoming re-established at the end of the melt season. The boundary conditions in our ice-dynamic solution remain fixed through time and we do not simulate the change in frontal forces as the lake drops during a flood; in effect, our Neumann boundary condition corresponds to a permanently empty lake (see Section~\ref{application:setup}). As a result we overestimate the background $v}_{\mamthrm{b}}$ into the lake (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}b) and underestimate the anomaly associated with lake drainage (Fig.~\ref{FIG:7}d). Nonetheless, our modelling does reproduce limited acceleration into the lake during a flood as a result of low effective pressures close to the ice--lake contact during a jökulhlaup. 


\subsection{Model limitations and future outlook}\label{discussion:limitations}

The primary discrepancy between our model and observations is that, as in the earlier 1D work \citep[e.g.,][]{kingslake2013modelling}, our experiments produce unrealistically long jökulhlaup durations extending multiple months. This stands in clear contrast to the days--weeks more typically associated with jökulhlaups \citep[e.g.,][]{bjornsson1992jokulhlaups,bjornsson2010understanding,einarsson2016spectrum,magnusson2026subglacial} and is much longer than the 5--8 day jökulhlaup duration observed at Isunnguata Sermia itself \citep{livingstone2025ice}. Results in Section~\ref{application:results} are presented from one model run at Isunnguata Sermia, chosen as the best match to lake level through time (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}); parameter testing was not exhaustive, and untested combinations may improve agreement with jökulhlaup duration. However, we expect the discrepancy to be attributable, at least in part, to limitations in our modelling setup (Sections~\ref{methods:GlaDS}--\ref{methods:Lake}) and with GlaDS more generally \citep{werder2013modeling}. 

As a continuum model, representing smoothly distributed drainage properties, GlaDS is not ideally suited to represent rapid, localised changes in subglacial water pressure or hydraulic connectivity \citep{rada2018channelized,hill2025emulator}. In ISSM-GlaDS, there is no physical representation of elastic uplift of ice once water pressure exceeds ice overburden pressure. This, together with our decision to prevent cavity expansion when effective pressure is negative (effectively fixing maximum sheet thickness to the basal bump height), means that our model is unable to replicate the instantaneous changes in sheet transmissivity expected once water pressure exceed ice overburden pressure \citep[e.g.,][]{tsai2010model,schoof2012flotation,dow2015modeling}. Work by \citet{flowers2004coupled}, albeit with a prescribed rate of flux from a subglacial lake, found that an elastic model able to represent a sheet thickness exceeding tens of metres can explain rapid escalations in jökulhlaup discharge over hours to days. Recent repeat satellite observations identified metres of uplift along a jökulhlaup pathway, suggesting that instantaneous ice uplift in a high water-pressure regime is crucial to enabling high jökulhlaup discharge \citep{magnusson2026subglacial}. A simple model of elastic deformation has been included in similar hydrology models elsewhere \citep[e.g.,][]{stevens2022tidewater}, and is worth exploring in future work. Here, with no physically-informed representation of rapid cavity expansion, our model is unable to reproduce this specific mode of fast flood onset and instead floods primarily evolve as a result of channel expansion. 

Additionally, we assume water in the subglacial system, and crossing the ice--lake contact, is always at pressure melting point (Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}). Previous work \citep[e.g.,][]{clarke2003hydraulics,ng2007climatic,carrivick2017ice} has established that high water temperature increases the heat energy available for channel expansion via wall melting, resulting in higher peak discharge and shorter duration floods \citep[see Fig. 6A in][]{carrivick2017ice}. Inclusion of this factor may simultaneously resolve our underprediction of flood peak discharge and our overprediction of flood duration (discussed above). This is because in our underlying hydrology solution, water temperature is assumed to remain at pressure melting point throughout the subglacial drainage system (see Section~\ref{methods:GlaDS}). In an analogous supraglacial lake setting, and using the \citet{spring1981numerical} equations for channel enlargement, \citet{werder2010hazard} found that increasing the lake temperature from 1$^{\degree}$C to 4$^{\degree}$C increases peak discharge from 380 $\mathrm{m}^3 \, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ to 960 $\mathrm{m}^3 \, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$. Without detailed field measurements of lake temperature in our Greenland experiment, we effectively assume that the temperature of water leaving the lake across the ice--lake contact is also at the pressure melting point. 

We also make other assumptions which influence our Greenland model outcomes and likely dampen the peak flood discharge. Lake area, $A_{\mathrm{L}}$ remains fixed in time, reducing the lake to a vertically-walled reservoir and ensuring that the scaling relationship between flood discharge and lake depth remains constant. This simplification likely biases our lake drawdown rates towards being too slow; in reality as a lake drains its areal footprint decreases, which would amplify the rate of lake level decrease during a jökulhlaup. In our ice dynamics model, we fix ice thickness through time. This was a deliberate modelling choice made in the absence of a detailed understanding of lake calving dynamics to avoid the unphysical build up of ice at the lake boundary where surface mass balance alone is unable to account for observed mass balance changes. As a result, we do not capture the decreasing trend in peak $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ over time (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}g), which is attributed to glacier thinning close to the terminus of Isunnguata Sermia \citep{livingstone2025ice}. Glacier thinning, and a reduction in $l_{\mathrm{h}}$ necessary to initiate flooding, may contribute to the short return interval of the 2023 flood \citep{livingstone2025ice}, which is notable as the only flood in the observed record without a corresponding match in the modelled record. Future work will seek to implement a calving law, to allow ice thickness evolution necessary to account for changes in $H$ through time. Finally, as described in Section~\ref{discussion}, our Neumann boundary condition in our ice dynamic solution at the western lake margin remains constant in time, effectively corresponding to a permanently empty lake. Recent work with a prescribed lake depth record has shown changing lake level can promote complex feedbacks in lake-terminating glacial systems as a result of the loading force a water body imparts on a vertical ice front \citep{pramanik2026modelling}. 

Our Greenland experiment parameters (Section~\ref{application:greenland}, Table~\ref{tab:Greenland}) provide additional insight into the limitations of our approach. Reproducing the amplitude and periodicity of lake fill--drain cycles (Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}f,g) requires relatively low sheet and channel conductivities; a low basal bump height; and a relatively high englacial void ratio. Each parameter falls within previously established bounds \citep[e.g.,][]{werder2013modeling,downs2018dynamic,hill2025emulator} but are collectively indicative of a hydraulically inefficient subglacial system with high englacial water storage. Physically, an inefficient system restricts channel development and ensures that jökulhlaups only trigger once the hydrostatic pressure at the ice--lake contact approaches $N=0\, \mathrm{Pa}$. The high englacial void ratio then buffers water pressure during flood propagation, producing weeks--months long flood durations (e.g., Fig.~\ref{FIG:5}e,f). We suggest that water storage as a result of the high englacial void ratio is partially compensating for the absent physics described above; elastic uplift and thermally enhanced channel melt would both act to accelerate jökulhlaup propagation. This reflects a broader challenge in applying continuum subglacial hydrology models to investigate glaciological observations. Parameter choices can compensate for missing physical processes and allow models to reproduce observed behaviour for the wrong physical reasons \citep{downs2018dynamic,hill2025emulator}. Here, specific parameters representative of restricted subglacial efficiency, are needed to match the periodicity of lake fill--drain cycles. Accordingly, we suggest that meaningful improvements in simulated jökulhlaup dynamics are more likely to follow from incorporating missing physical processes than from parameter optimisation alone.

Notwithstanding these limitations, and recognising that our model successfully reproduces flood timing across a multi-decadal record without tuning to individual events, we view the discrepancies in duration and peak as a tractable modelling challenge rather than a fundamental limitation. The framework presented here is readily extensible, and the lake model is parameterised by relatively few, geometrically-interpretable quantities (e.g., lake area, ice thickness at the ice--lake contact, and extraglacial inputs) meaning the model could be straightforwardly applied to other ice-marginal lake systems, including systems with multiple lakes, without modification of the numerical scheme. In addition, our results suggest that periodically draining ice-marginal lakes may represent promising alternative benchmarks for evaluating machine-learning-based emulators of current and future models of subglacial hydrology \citep[e.g.,][]{brinkerhoff2021constraining,verjans2024accelerating,hill2025computationally}. Ice-marginal lakes offer spatially integrated, remotely observable records \citep{domgaard2024altimetry,livingstone2025ice} that are less impacted by the substantial point-to-point variability that characterises borehole and moulin datasets \citep{murray1995black,rada2018channelized,ing2026connectivity} which have been previously used to evaluate emulators of subglacial hydrology \citep[e.g.,][]{hill2025emulator}.

\conclusions

We present an ice-marginal lake coupled to a 2D model of subglacial hydrology and ice dynamics, advancing beyond previous 1D models to include lateral flood propagation and coupling between the lake and distributed and channelised drainage components. Our model reproduces characteristic jökulhlaup cycles and patterns of basal sliding, but additionally shows that system-wide pressure changes are important controls on jökulhlaup timing and peak discharge. These water pressure changes, whether driven by basal meltwater availability or system efficiency, alter the `leakage' rate of the lake, and control whether drainage is routed via high discharge channels or is accommodated within the distributed system. Our model is applied to Isunnguata Sermia in west Greenland, where it successfully reproduces observed lake level fluctuations over a 17 year record, without tuning to individual jökulhlaup events. Predicted jökulhlaups remain unrealistically long and too low in peak discharge, which we attribute to missing physics, specifically, no representation of ice uplift and constant lake water temperatures. Nonetheless, we emphasise that our approach has produced results consistent with observations, is readily extensible, and is transferable to other glacier-lake systems.

\subsection{The Glacier Drainage System model}\label{Appendix:GlaDS}

As described fully in \citet{werder2013modeling}, the hydraulic potential at the bed, $\phi$, is defined at the bed by: 

\begin{equation}
\label{appeq:GlaDSdirBC}
\begin{aligned}
\phi = \phi_{\mathrm{m}} + p_{\mathrm{w}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

with water pressure, $p_{\mathrm{w}}$, and elevation potential

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:elevationpotential}
\begin{aligned}
\phi_{\mathrm{m}} = \rho_{\mathrm{w}}gz_{\mathrm{b}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $\rho_{\mathrm{w}}$ is water density, $g$ is gravitational acceleration and $z_{\mathrm{b}}$ is bed elevation. In turn, effective pressure $N$ is given by: 

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn}
N = p_{\mathrm{i}}-p_{\mathrm{w}} = \phi_{\mathrm{O}}-\phi,
\end{equation}

where $p_{\mathrm{i}}$ is ice overburden pressure ($p_{\mathrm{i}} = \rho_{\mathrm{i}}gH$) and $\phi_{\mathrm{O}}$ is the overburden potential ($\rho_{\mathrm{O}} = \phi_{\mathrm{m}}+p_{\mathrm{i}}$).

In the original GlaDS \citep{werder2013modeling}, sheet discharge, $q_{\mathrm{s}}$ is given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn:gladsqs}
\begin{aligned}
q_{\mathrm{s}} = -k_{\mathrm{s}}h_{\mathrm{s}}^{\alpha}|\nabla \phi|^{\beta}\nabla \phi,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $k_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the sheet conductivity; $h_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the sheet thickness; and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are flow exponents. Following the Darcy-Weisbach law, when flow is fully turbulent the flow exponents $\alpha=\mathrm{5/4}$ and $\beta=\mathrm{3/2}$. Here we use the \citet{hill2024improved} laminar--turbulent transition model, and with $\alpha_{\mathrm{s}}= \mathrm{5/4}$ as here, Eq~\ref{appeqn:gladsqs} becomes:  

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: Transition}
\begin{aligned}
q_{\mathrm{s}} = -\frac{\nu}{2 \omega} \left(\frac{h_{\mathrm{b}}}{h_{\mathrm{s}}}\right)^{1 / 2} \hspace{10em}\\
\hspace{3em} \times \left(-1 + \sqrt{1 + 4 \frac{\omega}{\nu} \left(\frac{h_{\mathrm{s}}}{h_{\mathrm{b}}}\right)^{1 / 2} k_{\mathrm{s}} h^3 |\nabla \phi|} \right) \frac{\nabla \phi}{|\nabla \phi|}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\omega$ is a laminar-–turbulent transition parameter (corresponding to the Reynolds number at which water becomes fully turbulent), $h_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the bed bump height, and $v$ is the kinematic viscosity of water at 0$^{\degree}$C. 

The cavity sheet thickness evolves through time given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: hs_cav}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial h_{\mathrm{s}}}{\partial t} = w - r,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

for functions $w$ and $r$, which describe the cavity opening and closing rate, respectively \citep{kamb1987glacier,walder1994channelized}. Cavities open at a rate given by basal velocity $U_{\mathrm{b}}$ over basal bumps with height, $h_{\mathrm{b}}$:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: basal sliding}
w(h) = 
	\begin{cases}
	U_{\mathrm{b}}({h_{\mathrm{r}} - h_{\mathrm{s}}})/{l_{\mathrm{r}}} & \text{if } h < h_{\mathrm{r}} \\
	0 & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
\end{equation}

where $l_{\mathrm{r}}$ is the horizontal cavity spacing. Viscous ice deformation leads to cavity closure, related to the effective pressure, $N$ by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: viscous closure}
\begin{aligned}
r(h,N) = Ah_{\mathrm{s}}|N|^{n-1}N, 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $A$ is the rheological constant of ice, multiplied by a first order geometrical factor ($h_{\mathrm{s}}$), and $n$ is Glen's flow law exponent. 

Sheet elements exchange water with channels, the cross-sectional of which, $S$, evolves through time as a function of an opening rate ($\Xi - \Pi/\rho_{mathrm{i}}L$) and closure rate $v_{\mathrm{c}}$

\begin{equation}
\label{apeq:channel cross section}
\frac{\partial S}{\partial t}=\frac{\Xi-\Pi}{\rho_{\mathrm{i}} L}-v_\mathrm{c},
\end{equation}
\noindent

where $\Pi$ is the rate of dissipation of potential energy per unit length of channel, $\Xi$ is the rate of change of sensible heat per unit length of channel, $\rho_{\mathrm{i}}$ is the ice density $L$ is the latent heat of fusion, and $v_{\mathrm{c}}$ is a channel closure rate.

Finally, channel discharge, $Q_{\mathrm{c}}$ is also related to the hydraulic potential gradient by:

\begin{equation}
\label{appeqn: Qc}
\begin{aligned}
Q_{\mathrm{c}} = -k_{\mathrm{c}}S^{\alpha_{\mathrm{c}}}\left|\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial s}\right|^{\beta_{c}-2}\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial s},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $k_{\mathrm{c}}$ is the channel conductivity; $s$ is the horizontal coordinate along a channel; and $\alpha_{\mathrm{c}}$ and $\beta_{\mathrm{c}}$ are channel flow exponents.
